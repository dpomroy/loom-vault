# Notes: Hyperliquid & Jeffrey Yan: Beyond the Sky - Colossus

## Summary

Hyperliquid is a **blockchain + derivatives exchange**
built by 11 people generating $900M profit in a year —
with no VC funding, no paid market makers,
and the largest user airdrop in crypto history.

Jeffrey Yan, 31, is the founder.
He traded anonymously from Puerto Rico,
then shut it all down to rebuild finance from scratch.

The platform's bet: **replace centralised intermediaries**
with public, programmable infrastructure
that anyone can build on and own.

What makes this unusual isn't the numbers.
It's the consistency of the philosophy —
no shortcuts, no insiders, everything on-chain —
applied at every decision point over three years.

The US market is still locked out.
That's the unresolved question.
Everything else is already bending.

## Quotes

### "I felt like I could go and build a thing that would revolutionize finance."

Yan found Ethereum's yellow paper — a computer
no single person could shut down —
while touching real finance every day at HRT.

Classic founder origin story, but the unusual
part is he already had the skills to
actually execute on it. Most people who say
this line never shipped anything.

---

### "There is a lot of sketchy behavior in the space. It may be healthy that people are realizing these things are not what they're advertised to be."

Yan doesn't defend the industry
he's built inside of.

Rare. Most founders in a sector
reflexively defend it.
The detachment here is consistent with someone
building infrastructure, not narrative.

---

### "If you're not the first person doing something, it's probably not worth your time to be doing it at all."

He extends this logic to reading —
if helpful context exists on what you're doing,
it's probably already been done.

Provocative and partially right.
The problem is it's a framework
that only works in hindsight —
you can't always know you're first until after.
Still, as a forcing function for originality
it's more useful than it sounds.

---

### "If Bitcoin had raised VC rounds, I really don't think it would be Bitcoin. Its entire value proposition would have been destroyed."

He turned down ~$100M at a $1B valuation.
His framing: Hyperliquid isn't a company —
it's a **protocol**, and neutrality from day one
was the whole point.

This is the decision that separates Hyperliquid
from every other crypto project.
It also meant Yan personally funded
the team's burn for over a year.

---

### "Alameda was essential to how FTX worked. We did not want HLP to be essential to how Hyperliquid works."

He built the community vault to bootstrap
liquidity only — then actively worked
to make it redundant.

Most founders treat their own infrastructure
as a moat. Yan treats dependency
as a structural risk. That's a different
mental model entirely.

---

### "I think people are just a bit too soft in general. The brain is an organ. If you need to work more hours, you can train for it."

He's learned not to impose this on the team —
but the belief is genuine and consistent
with someone who hit 100-hour weeks
alone in Puerto Rico.

Worth pushing back on slightly —
this framing ignores diminishing returns
on exhausted cognition.
But as a personal operating mode
for high-leverage frontier work,
the evidence is hard to argue with.

---

### "It's the largest wealth transfer in the history of cryptocurrency, and every dollar of it went to users."

31% of total token supply
to ~94,000 early users.
No conditions, no vesting.
At peak, worth **$16 billion**.
The team received nothing on launch day.
VCs received nothing.

This is the line that makes
everything else credible.
Anyone can say they're building
for users. This was verifiable,
on-chain, and irreversible.

---

### "It sucks to have to learn it from an attack rather than someone just telling you."

After the Jelly Jelly exploit cost
HLP $12M+, Yan acknowledged the gap —
but also noted Hyperliquid
will pay up to **$1M for a bug report**.

The gap between "open to feedback"
and "willing to pay $1M for it"
is where most companies live.
That's a meaningful commitment to transparency.

---

### "It's the hard way to do things, but we care about how we get to our goal, because how we get there determines what it actually is at the end that we've built."

He explicitly chose not to build everything
in-house — deliberately franchising out
the user layer so others could own
businesses on top of Hyperliquid.

This is the Amazon/AWS insight
applied to finance infrastructure.
The platform layer becomes more valuable
the less it tries to own the surface.

---

### "Be very confident you're going in the right direction, and execute well on the step you're taking right now, without knowing exactly where you're going."

He uses Go vs chess as the frame:
chess rewards reading further ahead,
Go rewards **intuition for the next move**.
Multi-decade goals, he says,
are "very presumptuous."

This is the most honest thing
a founder with a 20-year thesis
can say about how they actually work.
Direction over destination.

## Key Arguments

1. **Centralised crypto exchanges are the problem**,
   not the solution — Hyperliquid is the first
   decentralised platform to make a real dent
   in the perps market by building its own
   faster blockchain from scratch.

2. **Refusing VC money wasn't idealism —
   it was product integrity.**
   External capital would have
   compromised the neutrality of the protocol,
   the same way it would have compromised Bitcoin.

3. **The HIP-3 architecture is the real bet:**
   by letting independent teams deploy
   any market with a price feed,
   Hyperliquid is becoming financial infrastructure
   rather than just another exchange —
   oil, gold, S&P 500, prediction markets,
   all on a public, 24/7 ledger.

4. **The airdrop was a credibility event,
   not a marketing event.**
   $16B at peak, no insider allocation,
   on-chain and irreversible —
   it converted three years of stated values
   into a single verifiable act.

5. **The US regulatory block is the
   single biggest unresolved constraint.**
   Americans can't legally touch
   a platform doing $4T cumulative volume.
   That's the next mountain.
