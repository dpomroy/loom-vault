# Notes: How to Do Great Work

## Summary

Paul Graham's essay argues that **great work** follows a
repeatable pattern across all fields:
find what you're genuinely curious about,
reach the frontier of that field, notice
the gaps, then explore them.

The essay's core bet is that **curiosity is
the master variable** — not discipline,
not talent, not planning. It also makes a
sharp distinction between per-day
procrastination (obvious) and per-project
procrastination (invisible, lethal).

Relevant to Loom: the essay is essentially
an argument for building a personal
knowledge system around **genuine interest**,
not optimised relevance. The projects
you're already running — Loom, racing tool,
MT5 — are what Graham would call "working
on your own projects." That's the right
posture. The risk he'd flag is whether
you're working on the **most important**
version of those problems.

## Quotes

### "There's a kind of excited curiosity that's both the engine and the rudder of great work. It will not only drive you, but if you let it have its way, will also show you what to work on."

Curiosity as navigation, not just fuel.
Most productivity advice treats motivation
as the problem. Graham says curiosity
solves *selection* too — it tells you
*what* to work on, not just *how hard*.

Most people optimise for motivation after
picking a goal. Graham collapses those
into one step.

---

### "Knowledge expands fractally, and from a distance its edges look smooth, but once you learn enough to get close to one, they turn out to be full of gaps."

The implication: the frontier only becomes
visible once you're close enough.
This is why shallow knowledge produces
no original ideas. You can't see the gaps
from a distance — you have to go deep
enough that the surface texture changes.

Directly relevant to Loom: a feed built
for **depth** in a few areas will surface
real gaps; a feed built for breadth won't.

---

### "Per-project procrastination is far the more dangerous. You put off starting that ambitious project from year to year because the time isn't quite right. When you're procrastinating in units of years, you can get a lot not done."

The brutal version of this: it hides
behind busyness.

You're not sitting idle — you're
"working industriously on something else."
The alarm never goes off. The question
"Am I working on what I most want to work
on?" should probably be a recurring prompt
in Loom itself.

---

### "Originality isn't a process, but a habit of mind. Original thinkers throw off new ideas about whatever they focus on, like an angle grinder throwing off sparks. They can't help it."

This reframes originality as a state of
engagement, not a technique.

You can't schedule creative output.
You can only maintain the conditions —
deep focus on hard problems — that make
it a side effect. "Creative process" is
a comforting fiction; this is closer to
the truth.

---

### "A good new idea has to seem bad to most people, or someone would have already explored it. So what you're looking for is ideas that seem crazy, but the right kind of crazy."

The distinguishing test: ideas that are
the **right kind of crazy** tend to be
exciting and rich in implications.

Ideas that are *merely* bad tend to be
depressing. That's actually a
usable heuristic — not "is this weird"
but "does it feel alive or deflating?"

---

### "People show much more originality in solving problems than in deciding which problems to solve. Even the smartest can be surprisingly conservative when deciding what to work on."

Problem *selection* is where most smart
people leave the most value on the table.

This is the essay's sharpest structural
point. It's counterintuitive because
effort is visible and problem choice is
invisible. Most tools — including Loom
as currently conceived — optimise for
helping you *work*, not helping you pick
*what to work on*.

---

### "The trouble with planning is that it only works for achievements you can describe in advance. You can win a gold medal or get rich by deciding to as a child and then tenaciously pursuing that goal, but you can't discover natural selection that way."

Planning is a local maximum trap.

The projects worth doing — the ones that
**expand what's possible** — can't be
specified in advance. This doesn't mean
drift; it means preserving optionality
and following the most interesting next
step. Graham calls it "staying upwind."

---

### "Morale compounds via work: high morale helps you do good work, which increases your morale and helps you do even better work. But this cycle also operates in the other direction."

The practical output of this: when stuck,
switch to easier work to restart the
positive cycle.

This is underrated advice because it
sounds like giving up. It isn't.
Momentum is a real variable, not a
metaphor. Protecting it by doing
*something* is legitimate strategy.

---

### "Be professionally curious about a few topics and idly curious about many more."

Distribute attention like a power law —
not evenly.

This is directly a Loom design principle.
A feed that treats all topics equally is
wrong by default. The feed should reflect
a steep curve: deep on 2-3 things,
light-touch on many more.

---

### "Doing great work means doing something important so well that you expand people's ideas of what's possible."

A rare, clean definition buried in the
footnotes.

Most definitions of "great work" are
circular (great = recognised as great).
This one has a testable shape:
did it shift what people thought was
achievable? That's a meaningful bar.

---

### "Write the story you want to read; build the tool you want to use."

Classic Graham. Elegantly short.

The reason this keeps getting cited is
that it solves two problems at once:
it aligns your intrinsic motivation with
your output, *and* it gives you a
real user to build for (yourself).
Loom is exactly this kind of project —
worth remembering when it drifts toward
"what should a PKM app do."

## Key Arguments

1. **Curiosity is the master variable.**
   It selects the field, drives you to
   the frontier, flags the gaps, and
   sustains the effort — all four steps.
   Hard work matters, but interest beats
   diligence as a long-run engine.

2. **Per-project procrastination is the
   real threat.** It masquerades as
   productivity. The diagnostic question —
   "Am I working on what I most want to
   work on?" — needs to be asked
   deliberately and regularly.

3. **Problem selection matters more than
   problem solving.** Most smart people
   apply their originality to *solutions*
   and stay conservative on *which
   problems to pick*. The biggest
   leverage is choosing the right problem,
   especially an unfashionable or
   overlooked one.

4. **Planning is a trap for ambitious
   work.** It constrains you to outcomes
   you can pre-specify. The alternative
   is "staying upwind" — always making
   the choice that preserves the most
   interesting options, not executing a
   fixed roadmap.

5. **Great work requires earnestness —
   intellectual honesty as a capability,
   not just a virtue.** You can't see
   new ideas clearly if you're
   intellectually dishonest. Affectation,
   fashion, and orthodoxy are not just
   character flaws — they're epistemic
   handicaps that make discovery
   harder.
