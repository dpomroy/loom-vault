# Notes: How to Do Great Work

## Summary

Paul Graham's "How to Do Great Work" is a **synthesis of meta-principles** that cut across fields. The core claim: great work requires finding what you're genuinely, excessively curious about — then getting to the frontier, spotting gaps, and exploring them.

It's not a productivity guide. It's closer to a **philosophy of how to aim**.

Key conclusions up front:
- Curiosity is the single most important variable
- Per-project procrastination is the silent killer
- Planning is usually a trap — "staying upwind" beats roadmaps
- Originality lives in **choosing problems**, not solving them
- Morale is infrastructure, not a nice-to-have

Relevant to Loom specifically: the essay validates building tools you yourself want. That's not a compromise — it's the correct **signal-maximising strategy** for knowing if something's worth building.

## Quotes

### "There's a kind of excited curiosity that's both the engine and the rudder of great work."

The engine/rudder framing is precise in a way most motivation advice isn't. It does two jobs at once — it drives you forward and it points the direction. Most people treat these as separate problems.

Relevant to Loom: the app's whole premise is feeding curiosity better. This is the theoretical grounding for why that matters.

---

### "Knowledge expands fractally, and from a distance its edges look smooth, but once you learn enough to get close to one, they turn out to be full of gaps."

This is the best one-sentence explanation of why expertise feels discouraging at first and then accelerating. The smooth-from-a-distance illusion is what puts beginners off and keeps pretenders comfortable.

It also explains why generalist reading only gets you so far — you need depth to see where the real gaps are.

---

### "Per-project procrastination is far the more dangerous. You put off starting that ambitious project from year to year because the time isn't quite right. When you're procrastinating in units of years, you can get a lot not done."

The brutality of "get a lot not done" lands. The reason this form of procrastination is so dangerous is the next point: it disguises itself as productivity. You're always busy — just never on the thing that matters most.

The antidote PG offers: periodically ask "am I working on what I **most** want to work on?" That's a question worth scheduling.

---

### "One reason per-project procrastination is so dangerous is that it usually camouflages itself as work. You're not just sitting around doing nothing; you're working industriously on something else."

This is the version of the trap most ambitious people fall into. The alarm never goes off because surface-level you look productive. It's only visible in hindsight — or when you ask the question directly.

---

### "The trouble with planning is that it only works for achievements you can describe in advance. You can win a gold medal or get rich by deciding to as a child and then tenaciously pursuing that goal, but you can't discover natural selection that way."

The gold medal vs. natural selection contrast is the sharpest version of this argument I've seen. Planning optimises for known targets. The best work is usually in unknown territory — so plans are a ceiling, not a floor.

This validates the "staying upwind" strategy: preserve **optionality**, don't optimise for a predetermined destination.

---

### "If you're excited about some possibility that everyone else ignores, and you have enough expertise to say precisely what they're all overlooking, that's as good a bet as you'll find."

The qualifier "enough expertise to say precisely" is doing real work here. Without it this is just contrarianism. With it, it's a genuine edge. The combination of conviction + precision is rare — most people have one or neither.

---

### "Originality isn't a process, but a habit of mind. Original thinkers throw off new ideas about whatever they focus on, like an angle grinder throwing off sparks. They can't help it."

This reframes "how do I be more creative" as the wrong question entirely. You don't switch originality on for a session — you either have the underlying habit or you're building it. The angle grinder image is good: it's a **property of the material**, not the motion.

---

### "Indeed, if you think about it, a good new idea has to seem bad to most people, or someone would have already explored it."

The follow-on distinction between exciting-bad and merely-bad ideas is the practical payoff. Exciting-bad ideas are "rich in implications." Merely-bad ones are "depressing." That's a real heuristic — the emotional texture of a bad idea tells you something about whether it's the right kind of bad.

---

### "Doing great work is a depth-first search whose root node is the desire to. So 'If at first you don't succeed, try, try again' isn't quite right. It should be: If at first you don't succeed, either try again, or backtrack and then try again."

The CS framing earns its place here — it separates backtracking (useful) from abandoning the root (fatal). The distinction between giving up on an approach vs. giving up on the goal is one most people don't make explicitly. They either never backtrack or they backtrack all the way out.

---

### "People show much more originality in solving problems than in deciding which problems to solve. Even the smartest can be surprisingly conservative when deciding what to work on."

The asymmetry between problem selection and problem solving is underappreciated. Smart people apply all their creativity *within* the box, then accept the box as given. Loom is partly a bet that better inputs shift which problems feel visible.

---

### "Curiosity is the key to all four steps in doing great work: it will choose the field for you, get you to the frontier, cause you to notice the gaps in it, and drive you to explore them."

This is the essay's thesis restated as a checklist. Every step has the same root cause, which means if curiosity is compromised — by prestige-chasing, fashion, fear — the whole pipeline degrades. It's not one input among many. It's the load-bearing variable.

---

## Key Arguments

1. **Curiosity is the master variable.** Not discipline, not talent, not planning. Curiosity selects the field, drives you to the frontier, surfaces the gaps, and sustains the work. Everything else is downstream of it.

2. **Problem selection matters more than problem solving.** Most people apply originality within a given problem frame but accept the frame itself uncritically. Choosing the *right* problem — especially an unfashionable or overlooked one — is where the real leverage is.

3. **Per-project procrastination is the silent killer.** It masquerades as productivity. You're always busy, just never on the thing that would matter most. The only antidote is periodically asking the direct question: *is this what I most want to work on?*

4. **Planning is a trap for discoverers.** It only works for goals you can specify in advance. Real breakthroughs don't fit that model. The correct strategy is "staying upwind" — at each stage, do what's most interesting and preserves the most future options.

5. **Morale is infrastructure.** It compounds in both directions. Protect it deliberately: right colleagues, right audience (small is fine), right physical conditions. Don't let a single setback burst the balloon — build setbacks into your model of the process from the start.
