# Notes: How to Do Great Work

## Summary

Paul Graham's core argument: **great work is not mysterious** — it follows a repeatable pattern. Pick something you're genuinely curious about, learn enough to reach the frontier, notice the gaps, then explore them. The rest is morale management.

The essay is long but the payload is tight:

- **Curiosity** is the engine, not discipline
- The hardest step is picking what to work on — and schools actively make you worse at this
- Per-project procrastination (staying busy on the wrong thing) is the **silent killer**
- Originality in *choosing* problems matters more than originality in solving them

Relevant to Daniel because Loom is exactly this kind of project — built from genuine curiosity, solving a problem you personally have, small and iterable. The essay validates the approach.

---

## Quotes

### "There's a kind of excited curiosity that's both the engine and the rudder of great work. It will not only drive you, but if you let it have its way, will also show you what to work on."

The **rudder** framing is the key bit. Most productivity advice treats curiosity as fuel — Graham says it's also navigation. You don't steer and then go; the going is the steering.

---

### "One reason per-project procrastination is so dangerous is that it usually camouflages itself as work. You're not just sitting around doing nothing; you're working industriously on something else. So per-project procrastination doesn't set off the alarms that per-day procrastination does. You're too busy to notice it."

This is the most practically dangerous idea in the essay. **Busyness is the disguise.** Worth running a personal audit: are Loom, the racing tool, the MT5 pipeline all real work — or is one of them cover for not doing the hardest one?

---

### "In most cases the recipe for doing great work is simply: work hard on excitingly ambitious projects, and something good will come of it. Instead of making a plan and then executing it, you just try to preserve certain invariants."

**Anti-planning** as a serious position. The invariants framing is underrated — it's closer to how good systems actually run than roadmaps are. Relevant to how Loom gets built: protect the core properties, let the rest emerge.

---

### "Indeed, if you think about it, a good new idea has to seem bad to most people, or someone would have already explored it. So what you're looking for is ideas that seem crazy, but the right kind of crazy."

Logically airtight. If it looked good to everyone, it'd already be done. The question is how to distinguish **right-kind-of-crazy** from just-crazy — Graham's answer (exciting + rich in implications) is decent but not precise enough.

---

### "Originality in choosing problems seems to matter even more than originality in solving them. That's what distinguishes the people who discover whole new fields. So what might seem to be merely the initial step — deciding what to work on — is in a sense the key to the whole game."

Underrated point. Most people treat **problem selection** as preamble. Graham says it's the main event. The whole essay builds to this and most readers still miss it.

---

### "Morale compounds via work: high morale helps you do good work, which increases your morale and helps you do even better work. But this cycle also operates in the other direction: if you're not doing good work, that can demoralize you and make it even harder to. Since it matters so much for this cycle to be running in the right direction, it can be a good idea to switch to easier work when you're stuck, just so you start to get something done."

**Momentum is recursive** — in both directions. The practical advice (switch to easier work when stuck) is underrated. Most productivity culture treats this as quitting. It isn't; it's flywheel management.

---

### "Originality isn't a process, but a habit of mind. Original thinkers throw off new ideas about whatever they focus on, like an angle grinder throwing off sparks. They can't help it."

Good reframe. The **"creative process"** framing implies you can switch it on. Graham's version implies you either have the habit or you're building it — and building it happens through consistent focus, not technique.

---

### "But perhaps the worst thing schools do to you is train you to win by hacking the test. You can't do great work by doing that. You can't trick God. So stop looking for that kind of shortcut. The way to beat the system is to focus on problems and solutions that others have overlooked, not to skimp on the work itself."

Brutal and accurate. **Test-hacking instincts persist** long after school. Shows up as chasing metrics, optimising for looking productive, shipping things that score well on benchmarks but don't actually work. Directly relevant to the MT5 pipeline — don't overfit to backtest.

---

### "Curiosity is the best guide. Your curiosity never lies, and it knows more than you do about what's worth paying attention to."

Short but it's the thesis sentence of the whole essay. **Curiosity as epistemically superior** to deliberate judgment — because it aggregates signals you haven't consciously processed yet. Compelling if you accept it; slightly mystical if you don't.

---

### "Don't marry someone who doesn't understand that you need to work, or sees your work as competition for your attention. If you're ambitious, you need to work; it's almost like a medical condition; so someone who won't let you work either doesn't understand you, or does and doesn't care."

The bluntest thing in the essay. Graham treats **ambition as a constraint**, not a choice — which reframes the relationship question entirely. Either your environment accommodates it or it doesn't; there's no negotiating with physics.

## Key Arguments

1. **Great work has a repeatable structure**: choose field → reach frontier → notice gaps → explore them. Curiosity drives all four steps. The recipe is not "work harder" — it's "follow genuine interest past all obstacles."

2. **Problem selection is undervalued and underpracticed.** People apply originality to solutions but stay conservative about which problems to tackle. Choosing the right problem — especially an **unfashionable** one others think is exhausted — is where real leverage lives.

3. **Per-project procrastination is the primary threat.** It hides inside ordinary busyness. The fix is a periodic forced question: *Am I working on what I most want to work on?* Getting older makes the cost of a "no" higher.

4. **Morale is a system, not a mood.** It compounds in both directions. Protect it actively: control your environment, your colleagues, your physical state. A small dedicated audience beats a large indifferent one for sustaining the work.

5. **Earnestness — intellectual honesty + informality — is the prerequisite.** You can't do great work while performing a persona, chasing prestige, or optimising for how you appear. Any energy spent on seeming is subtracted directly from being.
