# Notes: Darren Farber on Iran, China, and the Rise of Neoprimes - [Invest Like the Best, EP.474]

## Summary

**Darren Farber** (Albion River, ex-DoD advisor)
argues that the US faces a structural problem:
fighting martyrdom-culture adversaries requires
**more moral compromise than democracies tolerate**.

- "Winning" in Iran means:
  strait open + military capability degraded
  enough it can't **reconstitute** quickly
- China is simultaneously strong *and* brittle —
  industrial mass vs. institutional illegitimacy
- **Neo-prime** defense companies need Congress
  to shift from one-year money to multi-year
  authorities, or capital markets dry up and
  the companies die before they matter
- Biggest underrated risk: AI models **poisoned**
  by adversary-planted fake data, corrupting
  military decision loops

Worth reading for the Eisenhower/Taylor doctrine
framing and the Hamas university co-option story —
both are historically grounded and not on your
radar if you haven't been tracking this space.

## Quotes

### "So winning is politically defined. The Strait of Hormuz was open before this contingency at the end of the day. And so if all we get is, is the Strait of Hormuz back open strategically, maybe was the juice worth the squeeze?"

Farber starts conclusion-first, which is rare in
this space. The framing forces the question most
analysts avoid: **what was the counterfactual**?
If the strait was already open, the bar for "win"
is higher than the administration is selling.

---

### "There's this hybridization that happened in certain parts of the Shia Muslim world through the works of Shiriadi, which is a hybridization of Marxism and martyrdom together... And to say if you martyr yourself, you're winning, you're ascending. It's such a satanical philosophy because it promotes internal destruction and sacrifice to the limit as evidence that you're winning."

This is the **structural problem** democracies
can't solve cleanly. You can't out-suffer a
martyrdom culture — the destruction *is* their
proof of victory. Changes how you think about
what "degrading capability" even means.

---

### "Dictators are enormously strong and enormously weak at the same time. This is actually like a good frame for our entire conversation, which is to say we have to be able to hold two competing ideas in the same vessel. And both can be true."

The strongest intellectual move in the episode.
Most people collapse to one view — either China
is unstoppable or it's a paper tiger. **Both are
true simultaneously** and strategy has to account
for that. Useful mental model beyond geopolitics.

### "They laid out in this single conversation that the FBI has transcripts of their plan to co opt universities and co op university teaching and institutions for the express purposes of changing Western opinion over the course of 20 years. I think Bezos has attributed the quote that if you have a seven year plan, you win because most people give up. These guys had a 20 year plan and it totally worked."

**Deliberately engineered**, not organic. The

### "You are constantly worried that someone's going to put a bullet in the back of your head and then that form of illegitimacy cascades down through the immediate organs of that government to include its military. China's **military corruption problem** is the
most underreported story in geopolitics. Turning
over senior leadership 3-4 times in three years
is extraordinary. An army that doesn't trust its
own generals has a serious coordination ceiling.

### "The rate of technological change in the commercial world becomes this direct input into the capabilities that you can project for mass. Drones have been used since the Vietnam War. But you wouldn't think that drones became commercially viable and cheap and by virtue of that became a new article of war. Because they were so inexpensive, the cost of losing one is low."

The **cost structure** is the real insight here,
not the drone technology itself. Cheap-to-lose
changes the calculus of deployment entirely.
Relevant to anyone building hardware for defence
— cost per unit matters more than peak capability.

This is the **kill condition** for neo-primes

## Key Arguments

1. **"Winning" against martyrdom cultures
   requires more violence than democracies
   have the political will to sustain** —
   because destruction is their proof of
   victory, not defeat. The Gaza/Hamas
   analogy is the working case study.

2. **China is strong and brittle simultaneously**
   — industrial mass and population scale on
   one side; deep institutional illegitimacy,
   military corruption, and leadership
   instability on the other. Strategic
   planning must hold both truths at once.

3. **Magazine depth (stockpile capacity) is the
   defining gap** in US defence posture —
   requiring a shift from one-year
   Congressional funding to multi-year
   contracts across ordnance and systems,
   or the industrial base can't rationally
   invest to fill it.

4. **Neo-prime defence companies will die
   from funding structure, not lack of
   product quality** — continuing resolutions
   block new starts, capital markets have
   limits, and without multi-year authority
   the best companies will exit before
   fielding systems of record.

5. **Commercial tech iteration speed now
   directly sets the pace of warfare** —
   Ukraine proved drones at 50+ iterations
   outpace any procurement cycle; the
   implication is the DoD needs to fund
   higher-risk R&D as a percentage of
   budget, closer to Manhattan Project
   levels in certain areas.
