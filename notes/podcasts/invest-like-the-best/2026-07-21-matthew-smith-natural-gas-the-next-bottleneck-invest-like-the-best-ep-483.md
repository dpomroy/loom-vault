# Notes: Matthew Smith — Natural Gas: The Next Bottleneck - [Invest Like the Best, EP.483]

## Summary

Matthew Smith, CIO of Chronometer Partners, argues that a **structural US natural gas deficit** is now mathematically locked in for the 2028–2030 window.

The core problem was already in place before AI arrived. LNG export commitments had consumed 100% of projected US supply growth. Then AI data-centre demand landed on top of a system with no slack left. By 2030, scheduled LNG exports alone could reach 35 BCF per day — before a single data centre is counted.

The constraint is not gas in the ground. It is **infrastructure build time**. Gathering lines, processing plants, and pipelines take 2–3 years to permit and construct. That lag makes the coming gap unavoidable regardless of how much resource exists underground. Storage exhaustion is the hard ceiling, and prices could reach levels the market currently treats as impossible.

Fifteen years of shale abundance has trained investors to ignore every tightness signal. The forward curve sits flat. Smith sees this complacency as the trade itself — when utilities begin hedging 2028 supply in earnest, he expects a sudden, violent scramble for physical gas.

The clearest winners are producers sitting on the best remaining rock and utility-scale solar operators, whose fuel cost is zero while the marginal price-setter rises sharply. The clearest warning is for anyone assuming fuel cells or SMRs will ride to the rescue — Smith is blunt that most remain science experiments, and that the gas to run them at scale may not exist in the system anyway.

## The Setup: LNG Locked It In

### "The die was cast long before AI COMPUTE came to the scene." [0:05:07]

The framing most people miss. The AI narrative is real, but Smith is saying the collision was already locked in through LNG export contracts signed years ago. AI is the accelerant, not the cause.

### "We're scheduled to export up to 35 BCF a day by the end of 2030." [0:00:00]

These projects are already financed, permitted, and well underway. This isn't a forecast — it's a contractual reality. The export ramp is the hidden load that most AI-focused energy analysis completely ignores.

## AI Adds a Second Shock

### "Starting in 2028, AI data centers and LNG exports will need more gas than the country can produce and deliver." [0:02:30]

The core thesis in one sentence. The market is pricing gas as if abundance continues. Smith is betting the curve is simply wrong, and the structural mismatch becomes undeniable around 2028.

### "If you plug in all of this compute and it's gas powered and we think gas could double or triple structurally. Even without weather, it could end up being 20 or 30% of the cost of compute by 2029." [0:39:41]

The energy cost shock is hiding in plain sight. Hyperscalers are building their P&Ls on a flat gas forward curve. That assumption is the load-bearing wall of their economics — and Smith thinks it cracks.

## Infrastructure Is the Real Bottleneck

### "A number of companies describe themselves as having a lot more inventory of wells to drill than we can justify with the facts." [0:00:00]

He's flagging that producer IR decks are overstating available inventory. Direct challenge to management credibility in the sector — worth verifying if you're looking at upstream names like EQT or Expand.

### "Many of the SMRs are still science experiments." [0:32:23]

SMRs remain unproven at scale. The hype is real but the manufacturing base isn't. Dismissing large-scale AP1000 for SMRs is a category error — you can't solve tens-of-gigawatts with science projects.

### "We are very cynical whether you can deploy fuel cells at scale because there isn't the gas in the system to power those 247365." [0:54:46]

Bloom Energy takes a direct hit here. Fuel cells as baseload generation aren't just uneconomic — they're physically constrained by the same supply crunch they're supposed to solve. Worth cross-referencing with the Caterpillar / Bloom loser thesis earlier in the episode.

## Storage, Prices, and the Hard Ceiling

### "The upside risk compressed in natural gas is both unbounded and convex." [0:04:51]

The price impact lands hardest in electricity — 2028, 2029, 2030. "Unbounded and convex" is doing a lot of work here. Smith is saying there's no ceiling once storage gets low enough, because there's no fast lever to pull.

### "Imagine being short memory a year ago or 18 months ago and finding out all of a sudden you're short memory. That is what this natural gas market looks like to us." [0:54:26]

The DRAM analogy is the sharpest framing in the whole excerpt. Slow build-up, then violent repricing. The people most exposed don't know they're short yet.

## Market Complacency as the Trade

### "Gas has lulled everybody to sleep." [0:23:35]

The complacency is structural: 15 years of abundance has created a flat forward curve that still shows $3.50–$3.60 out to 2030, even as storage starts breaking below all historical ranges from mid-2028. This is the asymmetric opportunity — the market isn't pricing what the well-level models show.

### "As soon as utilities turn the page and start to really hedge or buy gas in 28 and we start to see all these natural gas generating companies start to think about securing supply, you're going to start to see a knife fight to secure natural gas physical in 28 like we really haven't seen before." [0:52:08]

This is the trigger signal to watch. When utilities start hedging 2028 gas, the forward curve moves fast. Nobody has done this yet — which means the repricing event is still ahead of us, not priced in.

## Practical Advice for Capital Deployers

### "Make sure when your assets are deployed that you understand exactly what the source of your natural gas will be. Make sure you have physical supply locked up and that you understand your counterparties." [0:52:46]

The practical takeaway for any operator with energy exposure. Counterparty risk in gas hasn't been part of the AI-boom conversation at all — Smith's point is that it will be, and suddenly. Lock supply before the knife fight starts.
