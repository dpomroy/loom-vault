# Notes: Gavin Baker - Watts and Wafers - [Invest Like the Best, EP.473]

## Summary

**The two physical constraints on AI are power and silicon** —
and Gavin Baker thinks capitalism solves watts
by 2027–28, with orbital compute as the long-run fix.

**TSMC is the most important variable** in whether
AI builds into a bubble or compounds steadily.
Too much wafer supply = bubble. Too little =
Intel/Samsung break ranks anyway.

Baker is **broadly bullish but showing early
bubble-anxiety** — he sees a "diversity breakdown"
forming, where even former bears are now believers.

The **shift to usage-based pricing** is quietly
a bigger revenue catalyst than most investors
have modelled. And disaggregating prefill from
decode extends GPU useful lives to 10–15 years,
potentially saving private credit.

Key open questions: Will frontier tokens keep
commanding premiums? Does continual learning
arrive? Does the bitter lesson hold for 400+ IQ
models?

## Quotes

### "Anthropic added their combined businesses in one month. Nothing like that has ever happened in the history of capitalism."

The comparison is Palantir + Snowflake + Databricks — three companies, tens of thousands of employees, a decade each of building — matched in 30 days. The absolute scale of this claim is what makes it worth sitting with. Most investors saw the ARR print and moved on. Baker is saying this is a civilisational data point.

---

### "I became a believer and am a believer that I think maybe one thing that the market was mispricing... the Strait of Hormuz being closed is actually relatively awesome for America."

The logic: US electricity is gas-indexed, gas dropped 20% domestically while doubling in Asia and Europe — overnight manufacturing competitiveness gain. Contrarian read that most tech investors missed because they were anchored to 1970s energy-shock mental models. Worth stress-testing against your own macro assumptions.

---

### "If Taiwan Semi did what Jensen wanted, I think Nvidia could sell $2 trillion of GPUs in 26 or 27. Maybe two and a half trillion, maybe three trillion."

But unconstrained supply at that scale probably tips into overbuild. Baker's argument: TSMC's supply discipline is the single mechanism preventing a dot-com style crash. This is the load-bearing claim of the whole episode. If TSMC breaks or Intel/Samsung close the gap, the bubble thesis activates fast.

---

### "If we don't get a bubble, we need to throw a party for them because they will have single handedly prevented a bubble."

And the pressure valve is already leaking — companies are starting to go to Intel and Samsung for supply. The party line is darkly funny, but the point is serious: supply discipline from one Taiwanese company is all that stands between orderly compounding and a historic crash. That's a fragile system.

---

### "AI is just shifting from all you can eat to pay by the drink. And it turns out people really like to talk to their friends long distance, they really like to talk to their friends on the phone and people really like to use AI."

The cellular analogy lands well — usage-based pricing is why Baker thinks OpenAI and Anthropic could exceed $200B ARR this year. The flip side he buries: if you can't afford the enterprise plan, you're not at the frontier — which creates a compounding intelligence gap between well-funded and everyone else.

---

### "Claude even on opus is generating 70% less tokens for the exact same question... Token quantity equals quality of answer and quality of thinking at some level."

There's an intelligence density per token that also matters — it's not just volume. Relevant for Loom and any tool consuming AI outputs at scale. Rate-limited or capped plans aren't just slower — they're qualitatively lobotomised. This changes how to think about cost tiers when building on top of these APIs.

---

### "Prefill picture British naval ship from the 18th century. Prefill is loading the cannon, decode is firing."

Prefill = model understanding the prompt, fundamentally memory-capacity bound. Decode = generating new tokens, memory-bandwidth constrained. Clean mental model. The practical payoff is that disaggregating these two workloads lets you pair new inference chips with old Hopper/Ampere GPUs for prefill — extending useful life dramatically.

---

### "The disaggregation of pre fill and inference means that I think these GPUs are going to have 10 or 15 year lives."

Put a Cerebras or Groq LPU in front of an older GPU for prefill, run the older hardware until it melts. That changes GPU financing rates from low-7s toward 5–6%, materially lowering the cost of the entire AI buildout. This is an underreported structural shift. Private credit stress from SaaS loans may get partially offset by longer GPU asset lives — not obvious from the headline narrative.

---

### "My single most useful agent is a really good summary of the points that would be interesting to me from podcasts."

Six hours a day of content he feels obligated to watch — every major AI leader appearance — distilled to needles in haystacks. This is validation for Loom's core thesis from someone running a multi-billion dollar fund. The highest-value use case for a personal knowledge feed is exactly this: not summarisation for its own sake, but ruthless relevance filtering against a known person's information diet.

---

### "A violation of Richard Sutton's bitter lesson is for sure the biggest risk to this trade to all of AI."

The people closest to AI are the most skeptical this violation will occur. Baker is slightly less skeptical because he thinks we're close to ASI — and who knows if the bitter lesson holds for 400 IQ models. This is the honest bear case from a bull. More compute beating human ingenuity has held for decades. If ASI-level models flip that — even temporarily — the compute demand thesis inverts overnight.

---

### "The machine gun is here. If we do not all become masters of the machine gun, we're getting mastered."

The Last Samurai framing: experience and craft still matter, but only if you integrate the new technology. A seasoned investor using AI well will have advantages using the machine gun over someone who isn't. No hedging here. This is Baker's direct answer to "what do humans do now?" — adapt or be replaced, with the optimistic caveat that expertise compounds the tool's value.

---

### "There is this upsurge in political violence here in America and as AI increasingly becomes political, I worry that's going to get directed at more and more AI political leaders."

Baker flags a higher-variance, higher-beta world as a knock-on risk — including physical safety concerns for people publicly associated with AI. Unusual territory for an investment podcast. Worth noting that he's not being alarmist for effect — he's mapping second-order social instability as a real investment risk factor.

## Key Arguments

1. **Watts will be solved; wafers won't.**
Power constraints ease 2027–28 and orbital
compute (racks in space, connected by lasers)
resolves them long-term. Silicon is different —
TSMC's deliberate supply discipline is the only
credible bubble-prevention mechanism, and it's
fragile.

2. **Frontier tokens keep winning, and
usage-based pricing accelerates that.**
Despite open-source and distilled models,
economic value at the model layer has
overwhelmingly accrued to frontier inference.
The shift from flat-rate to consumption pricing
compounds this further — and creates a
widening capability gap for those who can't
pay enterprise rates.

3. **GPU disaggregation (prefill vs. decode)
extends useful hardware lives to 10–15 years**,
lowering financing costs for the entire AI
buildout and potentially rescuing private credit
funds underwriting GPU fleets on short
depreciation schedules.

4. **The bubble question hinges on TSMC
and the "diversity breakdown."**
Historical precedent says every foundational
technology produces a bubble. The current
build is different (cash-funded, 100% GPU
utilisation vs. 99% dark fibre in 2000) — but
Baker is seeing early signs of consensus
forming where even former bears are now bulls.

5. **Application layer value destruction is
the underreported story.**
Even counting the best AI-native companies,
AI has net-destroyed trillions in application
layer value. The companies creating the most
value have the highest **GPU-to-human ratio** —
a signal about where economic gravity is
actually sitting in the stack right now.
