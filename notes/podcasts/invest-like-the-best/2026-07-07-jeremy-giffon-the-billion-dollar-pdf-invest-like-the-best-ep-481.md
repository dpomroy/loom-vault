# Notes: Jeremy Giffon - The Billion Dollar PDF - [Invest Like the Best, EP.481]

## Summary

Jeremy Giffon argues that private markets
reward narrative over fundamentals,
and that most participants add complexity
to signal intelligence rather than generate returns.

The real edge is psychological clarity:
fund managers operating near their personal
wealth ceiling are compromised; founders
in zombie cap tables have more exits than
they know. Beneath all of it, Silicon Valley
runs on an unacknowledged quasi-religion —
and X's unified feed has handed cultural
power from billionaires to the poster class,
a new priestly caste shaping what gets
built and funded.

## Quotes

### "the great filter for funds is their storytelling ability, fundamentally because their product which is realized cash returns take a decade." [0:03:43]

The **interim product is pure narrative** — quarterly updates,
events, LP calls. Returns are the proof, but they come last.
Most managers never reckon with this honestly.

### "if you just changed the name and told a different story and just sort of arbitrarily started the clock two years ago, that company would actually be like really hot." [0:03:43]

This is a genuinely underrated structural arbitrage.
A **7-year-old company re-inflecting is priced as stale**;
the same metrics on a 2-year-old company get funded instantly.
The business is identical — only the story changed.

### "I've been shocked at sort of how hostile insider bridge rounds really are." [0:03:43]

Liquidation preferences, ratchets, warrants —
**extractive terms are tolerated when they're optimistic**.
A downside ratchet gets booed; an upside option at today's
price gets celebrated. Both steal from founders. Worth
remembering when reading any "supportive insider" bridge.

### "everyone gets served the same 500 tweets per day." [0:09:13]

The **uni-feed collapses opinion diversity** at scale.
Hundreds of millions of lurkers all reading the same thing —
and the poster-to-lurker ratio means a tiny group
is setting the agenda for everyone else.

### "posting is the last great meritocracy." [0:09:13]

Strong claim, but the **algorithm change makes it plausible**.
A new account with a clean post can now reach 500M people.
Old Twitter rewarded accumulated followings; new Twitter
rewards the post itself. The moat shifted from distribution
to quality — which is genuinely more meritocratic.

### "the billionaire class has become subservient to the posting class." [0:25:53]

The succession logic is clean:
**priests → scientists → billionaires → posters**.
You can always spot who's next by watching which class
starts deferring to the new one.
The Epstein anecdote about scientists chasing money
is the same pattern one level down.

### "I think you'll come to see billionaire as sort of this political label that's only tangentially related." [0:26:58]

The inflation argument alone deflates "billionaire" as a real wealth signal. It's become a vibe, not a number. Useful framing if you're building anything around wealth metrics or public perception of success.

### "The new scarcity is just like attention you can draw on the screen." [0:28:11]

Time is fixed — that's his anchor point. Everything else inflates away, so **attention** becomes the only real store of value. This reframes why rich people end up posting. Not ego — rational asset allocation.

### "One should not fool themselves that they are looking for anything other than entertainment in all the media that they consume." [0:31:11]

It's produced, selected, and edited to entertain. The whole "this podcast is educational" framing is just Rolex calling itself an investment. Relevant to Loom directly — worth being honest about what the feed actually *is* to users.

### "Probably the most enlightened way to consume this media is to not read it yourself." [0:31:11]

Let people in your network absorb the feed and surface what matters at dinner. Social filtering beats algorithmic filtering. Contrarian but hard to argue with. Also slightly undermines the personal knowledge feed concept — worth sitting with.

### "Every white collar job is totally fake and made up in the sense that these are not contingent for shelter and food and medicine." [0:35:53]

He's not being edgy — he's making a structural point. Most jobs exist because **capital needs somewhere to go**, not because the output is essential. Pairs well with his work-from-home observation: if two hours of real work fills a forty-hour week, AI isn't destroying jobs — it's exposing them.

### "To put it crudely, a bit of a Walmart effect in software — the future looks like low gross margins, razor thin net margins, huge scale." [0:49:47]

SaaS sold copies of strings at near-zero marginal cost. AI sells **compute** — you re-run the cost every inference. High margins were always downstream of that string model, and that model is done. Directly relevant if any Loom infrastructure decisions involve SaaS tooling or AI API cost assumptions.

### "I think a lot of investors are in the like feel clever, look smart game more than the money game." [1:05:28]

The cleanest diagnosis of why most investment processes are broken. If your edge is **impressing LPs**, you will systematically avoid the obvious trades that actually pay.

### "you probably want to be long Elon Musk that level of idea." [1:05:28]

The uncomfortable implication: **great ideas are simple**, and the intellectual apparatus around them is largely dressing. Investing media exists to make "long Elon" feel like a differentiated thesis.

### "he would basically come into his office with a yellow legal pad and you would write out your thesis on one page and then you would tell him what percentage of your net worth you're going to put in the deal." [1:05:28]

Rainwater's filter is brilliant because it **forces two things at once**: clarity of reasoning AND skin-in-the-game signalling. Neither is comfortable. That's the point.

### "I tried to write one that followed a very simple rule which was a obviously I was going to post it on Twitter and LinkedIn. Anything you post has to be a good standalone post." [1:08:18]

Job descriptions as **self-selecting filters** — not documentation. The hiring insight here is about disqualification, not attraction. Same logic applies to how you'd write a cold-pitch or a product landing page.

### "I don't care who you are when you're taking bets that are an order of magnitude larger than any amount of money you've ever had, there's just a psychological factor there" [0:57:47]

The **grip tightens** as the stakes grow relative to your net worth — and tight grip kills returns. This is a real edge for wealthy managers, not just a status observation.

### "tech views itself as the ultimate philanthropy is the business that you're building." [1:11:40]

This is Silicon Valley's **founding myth** and its blind spot simultaneously. The self-righteousness removes the corrective pressure that forced finance to translate gains into culture, art, and accountability.

## Key Arguments

1. **Narrative beats fundamentals** in private
   markets — story quality drives valuation
   more than underlying business metrics

2. **Underwriting managers** starts with
   personal finances — someone at 2-3x
   their net worth is psychologically
   compromised before a single trade

3. **Simplicity outperforms complexity** —
   elaborate trades are usually status
   signals dressed as rigour, not edge

4. **Zombie cap tables** trap founders who
   don't realise they have more exit and
   restructuring options than assumed

5. **Silicon Valley's philosophy** is an
   invisible substrate — neo-Buddhist
   utilitarianism + EA + quasi-religion —
   shaping what gets built and why

6. **Poster class has replaced billionaires**
   as cultural priests, driven by X's
   unified algorithmic feed
