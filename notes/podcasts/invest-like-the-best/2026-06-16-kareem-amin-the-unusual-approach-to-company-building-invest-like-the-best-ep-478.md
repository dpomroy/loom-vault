# Notes: Kareem Amin - The Unusual Approach to Company Building - [Invest Like the Best, EP.478]

## Summary

Kareem Amin, CEO of Clay, argues that **real risk** is rarer than founders admit. Most people who call themselves risk-takers are quietly hedging. True risk means genuine uncertainty — you don't know what happens next, and failure would actually shame you.

He draws a sharp line between building from insecurity and building from wholeness. The chip-on-shoulder model produces clouded thinking and collateral damage to the people around you. A cleaner foundation is **self-respect** — the only North Star with a terminal state, unlike money or prestige, which can never be fully satisfied.

Inside Clay, this philosophy shapes how they allocate attention. They deliberately over-invest in things most companies undervalue: brand, content, and community. They hire for those roles like product managers, not support staff.

On people, Amin pushes back on the "fire fast" default. When someone is floundering nine months in, Clay treats it as a diagnostic puzzle first. He says honest intervention turns struggling hires into stars roughly half the time.

The product vision follows the same logic. Clay is building a guitar, not a microwave — six strings, twelve frets, but a lifetime of depth. As AI makes raw execution cheap, emotional resonance and mastery matter more, not less. On vision itself, he is equally direct: if you don't have one, don't perform one. Performed certainty misleads everyone behind you. Let vision emerge from honest action. And when something has run its course, let it end — scaling a zombie is worse than a graceful close.

## What Real Risk Actually Means

### "I think capitalism rewards risk more than anything else. I think a lot of people think it's like meritocracy or hard work, which I think is silly." [0:10:59]

Kareem's framing here is blunt and correct. Most startup culture conflates effort with reward — Kareem doesn't. The implication for anyone building something: optimise for **genuine risk-taking**, not the performance of it.

### "For me, at least, my definition of risk, or how I've experienced it is you need to not know what's going to happen. Genuinely, you're going to discover something new." [0:12:48]

His definition adds a second condition most people skip: the failure has to carry real **shame-potential**. That's a useful filter. If you're not slightly embarrassed by the possibility of failure, it probably isn't real risk.

## Self-Respect as North Star

### "The only judge of anything is yourself and your own self respect. You have to be doing it so that you feel like what you've done is interesting and worthwhile." [0:16:09]

External validation is structurally broken as a goal — ambitious people hold themselves to a higher standard than anyone else can, because they have full information. This connects directly to Daniel's "quality over speed" instinct: the gap between brilliant and crap is zero, and only you know which side you're on.

### "I'm not going to create from a place of lack anymore." [0:18:36]

A deliberate shift, not a passive one. His counter to the "chip on shoulder" VC trope: you **can** create from wholeness, and you'll cause less damage doing it. Worth interrogating which of your own projects are driven by lack vs. genuine interest.

## Anxiety, Scarcity, and the Future

### "Whenever you're picturing the future or anticipating the future, it's kind of like Freud's death drive. The only thing that happens in the future is you die." [0:27:25]

The insight snapped him back into his body and into **present boredom** — which he describes as delicious. Provocative reframe. Most productivity culture is essentially a death drive dressed up as ambition.

### "I have an accumulation tendency where I'm like, where's the food going to come from? And in the company I noticed, I'm like, where's the next ARR going to come from." [0:27:25]

Recognising the scarcity reflex — in food, in revenue — let him **take more risk** by not hoarding beyond actual need. For Loom or the trading pipeline: worth asking whether any "safety-first" decisions are actually accumulation anxiety in disguise.

## Over-Investing in Undervalued Roles

### "we over invest in things that people typically underinvested. We've over invested in recruiting, in brand, in content, in community building." [0:30:11]

The contrarian move isn't a secret tactic — it's just
**funding the things everyone else treats as overhead.**
Clay's brand reputation didn't happen by accident.

## Diagnosing Struggling Hires

### "Everybody talks about hiring fast and firing fast. We've had people who nine months in, were floundering and were like, this person is excellent." [0:30:11]

The "fire fast" dogma is lazy pattern-matching. Kareem
is arguing the **bottleneck is often the context**, not
the person — and that distinction takes time to
diagnose. Most managers won't do the work.

## Building Depth, Not Utility

### "we're building a guitar. With a guitar, there's just six strings and 12 frets. But you could spend your whole life just getting better at that." [0:39:58]

This is the **depth vs. commodity** framing in one image.
The microwave/guitar split is a useful test for any
product decision: are you adding features or deepening
the instrument? Directly relevant to how Loom's feed
mechanics should be designed.

### "I think this idea of accepting more sounds as music is actually a helpful product principle." [0:39:36]

The "**Emancipation of Dissonance**" frame is
surprisingly useful: what behaviour or workflow do you
currently dismiss as off that would actually work given
the right context? Worth running as a deliberate prompt
when evaluating new feature ideas.

## Vision: Perform Nothing You Don't Have

### "if you don't have a vision, then you don't have a vision. If you do have a vision, then you do have a vision. I think it's important to not say something that actually isn't happening." [0:41:37]

Blunt and correct. The startup world **fetishises the
origin myth** — the visionary founder who always knew.
Kareem is calling that out as retrospective fiction that
actively misleads people who are still figuring it out.

## Knowing When to End Things

### "maybe we can institutionalize these things rather than trying to scale things that become zombies or become worse versions of their former selves" [0:52:19]

The **death doula for companies** idea is genuinely
underexplored. Nobody has built the institutional
infrastructure for graceful company endings because the
VC incentive structure makes that conversation almost
impossible to have.

## Key Arguments

2. **Creating from wholeness** beats the chip-on-
   shoulder model — cleaner thinking, more customer
   focus, less collateral damage to people around you.

3. **Self-respect** is the only North Star that
   has a terminal state; money, prestige, and
   adulation can never be fully satisfied.

4. **Over-invest in undervalued roles** — brand,
   content, community — and hire those people
   like PMs, not support staff.

5. **"Fire fast" is often wrong.** Diagnose the
   role and context first; honest intervention
   turns struggling hires into stars ~50% of the time.

6. **Depth over utility.** A guitar beats a microwave
   — emotional resonance and mastery matter more
   as AI makes raw execution cheap.

7. **Vision is not a prerequisite.** Performing
   certainty you don't have misleads everyone
   behind you; let vision emerge from honest action.
