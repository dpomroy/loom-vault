# Notes: Alex Sacerdote - How to Invest Through Technology Cycles - [Invest Like the Best, EP.477]

## Summary

Alex Sacerdote's central claim is that **AI adoption is still at roughly 10 basis points** of its eventual S-curve — we are at the very beginning, not the middle.

That framing drives everything else. Most investors price the next quarter. Sacerdote models two to four years out, buying low-PE names before earnings compound into visibility. He cites Andy Grove: at true inflection points, data lags intuition, so you have to act before the numbers confirm you.

His highest-conviction near-term proof point is **coding**. There are 20 million coders globally, making it a $500 billion market before non-coders even enter. Andrej Karpathy's experience — where AI went from writing 20% of his code to writing all of it — is the template. This is labour replacement, not augmentation.

That distinction matters for incumbent software. CIO budgets are already shifting to AI tokens for faster ROI, and that happens before any legacy product visibly fails. The threat is structural and invisible until it isn't. The application layer remains too unsettled for Sacerdote to back yet — the boundary between foundational model and app is still moving.

On hardware, the opposite is happening. Workloads growing 10× annually push memory, cooling, PCBs and networking to physical limits, turning commodity businesses into ones with real moats. He sees CAGRs moving from 5% to 35–50% with rising margins.

His research edge is human and compounded. 2,500–3,000 face-to-face meetings a year, senior analysts averaging 10-plus years at the firm, and 20 years of institutional memory. Philip Fisher's scuttlebutt method, not AI document analysis, is still how he gets ahead of 95% of generalist PMs.

## AI Is at the Starting Line

### "The world doesn't think exponentially, and they're so focused on the next year, the next quarter. Very few people believe you can accurately predict two, three, four years out." [0:22:00]

This is the actual edge being described — not information advantage but *time horizon* advantage. Buying Nvidia at 4× earnings in 2023 wasn't genius data access; it was willingness to model further out than the consensus would.

### "Andy Grove says when you have strategic inflection points, you can't trust the data. Strategic inflection points are about intuition, anecdotal evidence." [0:25:27]

Strong counter-intuitive point for a quant-heavy era. At true inflection points, the data *lags* the reality by definition — the boy in China with the big phone is the signal, not the spreadsheet. Relevant for how to think about early AI adoption data right now.

### "It's okay to miss the first hundred percent. Peter Lynch said, white out the chart. It's all about the future." [0:27:15]

Psychologically the hardest thing in growth investing — anchoring to a past price you missed is how you stay out of multi-baggers. The S-curve framework gives you permission to buy late *because* you can see how far the runway still goes.

## Coding as Labour Replacement

### "The big kicker was code, and this is the true unlock of AI." [0:07:07]

The framing here is important — even a bull wasn't sure AI could replace labour early on. Coding is the first domain where AI demonstrably *does* the work rather than assists. Worth watching whether the same flip happens in other high-skill domains.

### "If you think about how many coders there are in the world, 20 million. You've got a half a trillion dollar market just from coding alone." [0:08:15]

$100/day per power user × 20M coders — rough but the order of magnitude is hard to argue with, especially on 7-9 month old tech. The real upside is the non-coders who'll enter the market. That second wave isn't in this number at all.

### "Karpathy said last year its code tools could write 20% and 80% would be handwritten. That flipped when the latest model came out and now he hasn't written a line of code except in English." [0:08:50]

Karpathy is about as credible a signal as you get here — this isn't a pundit flip-flopping, it's a practitioner reporting observed reality. The 20/80 → 0/100 shift happened inside 12 months. That's not a gradual S-curve — that's a step function.

## Legacy Software Under Threat

### "The old way of software is like using pen and paper or it's like a horse and buggy. The new way of software is like a jet engine or frankly the transporter from Star Trek." [0:38:12]

The framing is stark on purpose. If you believe it, traditional software incumbents aren't just slow — they're a **different category** of thing. That's a sell signal, not a hold.

### "Even if AI is not going to be disruptive, they're spending it on anthropic tokens because there's faster ROI there." [0:38:12]

This is the underrated headwind. Software companies lose **budget share** even in the scenario where AI doesn't disrupt them. Two problems, not one.

## Hardware De-commoditising

### "Now you go to AI, the workloads are growing 10x every year and they're pushing every single aspect of this hardware to the physical limits of what it can do." [0:44:41]

The **decommoditisation** thesis in one sentence. Every boring part of the server stack — memory, PCBs, networking — is suddenly innovation-constrained again. That's where the alpha is hiding.

### "You've gone from a 5% grow or low margin to a 35%, 40, 50 top line CAGR for the next four years with rising margins on top of that." [0:44:41]

He's talking about PCB suppliers and copper clad laminate — the most unglamorous part of the stack. **Boring hardware** with software-company growth rates is exactly the kind of mispricing that doesn't last long.

## Application Layer: Too Early

### "The AI can be a great reporter. It can't pick into the future." [1:00:29]

Clean, honest line. AI handles the **blocking and tackling** — notes, quarter reviews, getting up to speed fast. The synthesis layer — what changed, what does it mean for the thesis — stays human. Relevant for how Loom should position itself.

### "It takes a hundred people, 100 diversified PMs to realize Google's not a loser, it's a winner. And can we figure that out before 95% of those generalists PMs?" [1:07:32]

The **large-cap alpha** argument is counterintuitive but logical. More capital required to move consensus = more lag = more edge for the specialist. Institutions are structurally underweight mega-cap tech, which is the opportunity.

## Research Edge: Human at Volume

### "In tech, you gotta go out and talk to people." [1:08:17]

The bluntest line in the excerpt. Buffett's reading-and-thinking approach doesn't transfer to tech — the knowledge is too tacit, too fast-moving. Primary research at scale is the actual edge here.

### "We do 2,500 3,000 face to face meetings with management teams." [1:08:17]

That's a serious number. Roughly 125–150 meetings per analyst per year. Volume alone isn't the point — the **compounding** of pattern recognition across those meetings over two decades is.

### "Munger and Buffett talk about compounding knowledge. We've been compounding that knowledge for 20 years." [1:08:17]

The deliberate echo of Buffett/Munger language is strategic. Sacerdote is arguing that the moat isn't capital or process — it's **accumulated context** that can't be replicated quickly by a newer fund.

### "That research engine can support all these products and it's the same people that do Publix and the Private." [1:08:17]

One team, multiple vehicles. Operationally efficient, but worth watching — shared research across public and private books can create **conflict-of-interest** dynamics if incentives drift.

### "He said, I've been at Goldman for 41 years. How about I come and join you?" [1:09:19]

The detail that lands here isn't the sentiment — it's that a Goldman senior partner voluntarily subordinated himself to back his son's early-stage fund. That's an unusual vote of confidence in someone unproven.
