# Notes: John Kim - How to Raise a Few Billion Dollars - [Invest Like the Best, EP.482]

## Summary

John Kim's central argument is that **trust** — not logic or better data — is what ultimately closes capital commitments.

He draws a sharp distinction between belief and trust. A prospect can believe your strategy works and still say no. What moves money is consistency and reciprocity over time, not a more persuasive deck.

Kim's model of persuasion is simple: desire minus fear. But he stresses that the desire is rarely just returns. Many LPs aren't even compensated on performance. Their real motivations are status, access, mission, or belonging — and your job is to find that, then eliminate their specific fear rather than your imagined one.

Three practical laws give the framework shape. **Differentiation** = (track record + edge) divided by complexity — if an LP can't explain your strategy to their committee, you've already lost. Trade-offs mean you must pick your constraints and defend them. Pipeline is just arithmetic: pipeline × conversion ratio × bite size, nothing more.

The committee dynamic is where most fundraisers fail. Big capital moves through rooms you'll never be in. Kim's image for this is the Secretary of State — someone whose message travels intact without them present. Logic, he argues, is not the input to a good pitch; it's the output, the story a prospect tells themselves after the emotional decision is already made.

He closes with scarcity. Fake urgency destroys credibility instantly because experienced investors always recognise it. Genuine scarcity — a round that is actually closing — moves even large institutions. Speed of close, like everything else in fundraising, is a function of trust.

## Trust Closes Capital

### "Money Moves at the Speed of Trust" [0:03:06]

The whole book distilled into five words. Elegantly captures why a perfect pitch deck still fails — the **logic landed but the trust didn't**. Everything else in this episode is annotation on this sentence.

### "So many people do such a good job of using logos or logic to actually get somebody to a yes, and they still say no. Because it's the difference between belief and trust." [0:03:06]

The belief/trust gap is the most underrated concept in fundraising. Belief is intellectual; trust is emotional. You can win the argument and still lose the room. **Most pitches optimise for the wrong one.**

### "You can win the belief this plane's not going to crash. I'm still scared money's not going to move." [0:34:17]

This is the gap most pitches fall into.
Belief and trust are not the same thing —
and most people stop at belief.

Contrarian and probably correct. The implication: **build enough trust and you don't need to overcome risk tolerance** — you dissolve the perceived risk entirely. Relevant to how you frame anything speculative to a cautious audience.

### "The logic actually is an output of a successful sales pitch, not the input." [0:34:17]

Solid framing.

## Laws of Differentiation

### "Great differentiation requires great sacrifice." [0:22:24]

The VC weapons example that follows is the sharpest illustration — firms that said "never" and then quietly reversed course. A principle that applies well beyond fundraising: **consistency is the proof that your differentiation is real**, not branding.

### "It's literally just pipeline times conversion ratio times bite size. That literally is the only math that is important for a fundraiser." [0:27:56]

Strips the mysticism out of fundraising and turns it into a mechanical process. Once you're past your hard reelect number, **it's an effort problem, not a quality problem**. Just as applicable to sales pipelines generally.

## Scarcity: Real vs. Fake

Fake scarcity is one of the most common fundraising mistakes, and it's self-defeating. Once you lose trust on a small thing like this, you've **destroyed the velocity of money** — the whole mechanism you were trying to use.
