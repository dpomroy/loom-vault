# Notes: Dara Khosrowshahi - Uber's Bet on AVs, AI, and Building a Super-App - [Invest Like the Best, EP.476]

## Summary

Dara Khosrowshahi took the Uber CEO job because a friend reframed the question — not "will this make you happy?" but "where's the **impact**?" That shift in framing set the tone for everything that followed.

His method for fixing a broken company is engineering, not inspiration. He decomposes chaos into its component parts, fixes each dimension separately, then reassembles. Overthinking, he argues, makes things worse — action on a simplified problem beats paralysis on a complex one.

The strategic core of Uber is **supply aggregation**. Uber recruits drivers, fleets, and restaurants; demand follows automatically. This identity shapes how the company thinks about autonomous vehicles — not as a threat, but as another supply source to absorb. AVs on Uber's network run 30% busier than those operating alone, which is the proof point Dara uses to argue AV makers should partner rather than go it alone. Uber wants to own the infrastructure layer: depots, charging, insurance, and demand routing.

The biggest risk to that future isn't technical. It's social and regulatory — whether the public accepts AV technology, whether it stays accessible beyond wealthy users, and whether driver communities see it as additive rather than destructive. Uber's counter-narrative rests on data showing driver numbers are still growing.

Uber One, with 50 million members growing 50% year-on-year, is the loyalty moat that compounds all of this. On leadership, Dara is blunt: authority is a listening trap. The more power you hold, the less unfiltered signal reaches you. He actively seeks out troublemakers and treats being wrong as his primary source of learning.

## Decomposing Chaos Into Fixes

### "The most important how is simplifying the situation and breaking down what seems like an unassailable problem into its component parts." [0:07:05]

Vector mathematics applied to chaos: decompose a
three-dimensional mess, solve each axis, reassemble. This
is just good engineering discipline applied to leadership. Nothing
exotic — but the discipline to actually *do it* under pressure is
rare.

### "Overthinking things doesn't help. It only makes things worse." [0:09:36]

His stress management: list the problems, build an
approach, test and learn — no rumination. The immigrant

## Uber as a Supply-Led Company

### "We are a supply led company... if we do that, the demand will take care of itself." [0:22:55]

At Expedia, demand came first; at Uber,
**supply is recruited first** — drivers, restaurants, grocers —
and demand follows. This is the load-bearing strategic
insight for the whole AV discussion. Whoever locks up AV supply
partnerships earliest controls the market; Uber is betting that's
the moat.

### "for a driver who's on the app six hours a day, we are getting much better. We've got to put ourselves in the shoes of our merchant partners, of our delivery partners." [0:41:49]

The **P95 bug** framing is the sharp part here — a rare consumer glitch is a weekly event for a full-time driver. Good reminder that supply-side UX is a distinct and harder problem than consumer UX. Applies directly to Loom's courier-side thinking too.

## The AV Partnership Thesis

### "AVs that are on our network are 30% or more busy than... 1P AVs who aren't using our network." [0:25:16]

That 30% in trips and revenue per vehicle per day can make a
**huge difference** in ROI on expensive cars. This is
the number that justifies the whole partner-not-build strategy.
If true at scale, no AV manufacturer can afford to ignore Uber's
network — which flips the power dynamic neatly in Uber's favour.

### "we are going to have some players, for example, a wave who's focused on building one end to end model that they license to OEMs. And we will be responsible essentially for all the demand." [0:28:12]

The demand-aggregator thesis in one sentence. Uber doesn't need to win the AV tech race — it just needs to be the distribution layer every fleet plugs into. Classic platform logic.

### "the number of drivers joining the platform is increasing because it looks like AVs are actually adding incremental demand to the platform." [0:29:58]

Counter-intuitive early signal — AVs expanding the pie rather than cannibalising driver earnings. If this holds at scale it defuses the biggest regulatory objection. Big "if."

## Social and Regulatory AV Risk

### "it's terrific technology, it is going to be safer than human beings. But how does it interact with emergency service providers? How do we make sure that the technology is available to everybody, not just the wealthy." [0:29:58]

Dara naming the real pre-mortem risk: public legitimacy, not engineering. The technology works; the politics might not. Worth watching how Uber positions itself as the "fair access" face of AV rollout.

## Loyalty Moat: Uber One

### "the math is simple, which is you want to make sure that your costs grow slower than your revenue and good things happen over a long period of time." [1:01:05]

Dara's capital allocation framing.

Dressed up as insight but it's basic. Worth noting because great operators keep returning to obvious fundamentals — the discipline is in actually doing it.

### "I prioritize growth. I prioritize innovation over buybacks. If you're building the company right, you'll do both." [1:01:05]

His explicit priority ranking.

Clean and defensible. The "you'll do both" is the real claim — most companies face a real trade-off, so this is either confidence or the luxury of Uber's current cash position.

## Staying Close to Unfiltered Signal

### "it's the filtering that gets the edge out of the story or out of the situation. And it's often the edge that gives you an edge, not the average." [0:54:11]

Barry Diller's information philosophy distilled. Going to the source, not the summary, is the same instinct behind raw data access in trading pipelines. Processed information is lossy by design.

### "I'm looking for those troublemakers constantly. And that requires random interactions that are less efficient than the structured processes that have been put in front of me." [0:59:46]

The **mutation** metaphor is genuinely useful — cultural monocultures don't evolve, they stagnate. The deliberate inefficiency (random interactions over structured reporting) is the mechanism, not just the philosophy.

## Authority as a Listening Trap

### "the more successful you are, the more you tend to talk and the less you tend to listen." [1:03:04]

A trap he's clearly watched peers fall into.

This is the most reusable line in the excerpt. Applies well beyond CEOs — anyone building with authority faces this drift.

### "authority doesn't mean that you're right, but so many people do." [1:03:04]

The subtle version of the same point.

Quietly sharp. The word "do" carrying all the weight — they act *as if* authority confers correctness. He doesn't.

### "If I'm not wrong, if I'm not making mistakes, it's just not very interesting." [1:03:04]

His stated relationship with being wrong.

Sounds rehearsed but the Barry anecdote before it earns it. The framing of **boredom** as the cost of being right all the time is the interesting angle — not humility, just curiosity.
