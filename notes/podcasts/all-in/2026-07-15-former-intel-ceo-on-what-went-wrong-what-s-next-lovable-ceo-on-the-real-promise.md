# Notes: Former Intel CEO on What Went Wrong, What's Next + Lovable CEO on the Real Promise of Vibe Coding

## Summary

Intel's decline is a warning about what happens when **technical leadership** gives way to financial management at the top of a technology company.

The former CEO traces the rot directly: in the five years before his return, Intel returned $100 billion to shareholders instead of investing in fabrication. That pattern — buybacks over fabs, MBAs over engineers — compounded quietly for a decade before the damage became visible.

Taiwan sits underneath all of this as a structural vulnerability. The island holds three weeks of energy reserves, which means a blockade is a usable economic weapon requiring no military action at all. The CHIPS Act moves the needle — leading-edge domestic production rose from 12% to 18% — but that is movement, not safety.

The second half of the episode pivots to **vibe coding** and the company Lovable. The numbers are hard to dismiss: one million projects built per week, fifty million apps created in twenty months, with non-technical founders running real businesses on a $25/month plan. A build that would have cost $500,000 two years ago now takes four hours.

The deeper shift is structural. Companies are pulling out ten or more SaaS tools and replacing them with custom software built on top of APIs they already use. The bottleneck has flipped. Engineering capacity is no longer the constraint. The scarce resource is now human judgment — knowing what to build, which experiments to run, and which strategic bets actually matter. Better models do not solve that problem.

## Intel's Leadership Went Wrong

### "I view one of the things that went off the rail was when it started to be run by business people." [0:02:32]

The core diagnosis from someone who was there. Technical decisions at billion-dollar scale can't be made through a spreadsheet — Gelsinger's point is structural, not personal. The same pattern is playing out now at any tech company where finance runs product roadmap.

### "In the five years, five, six years before I came back, Intel gave $100 billion to shareholders." [0:03:20]

A staggering capital allocation failure. That money could have funded new fabs, EUV machines, or a GPU competitor to Nvidia. Instead it was handed back. The opportunity cost here is one of the great corporate case studies in short-termism.

## Taiwan's Economic Fragility

### "The economic impact of a brownout of Taiwan is greater than the Great Depression in the world." [0:16:33]

Taiwan has under three weeks of energy reserves. A blockade doesn't need missiles — just cutting LNG ships for 21 days browns out the fabs, and a fab that goes dark takes 90 days to restart. This reframes the Taiwan risk entirely: it's an **energy logistics** problem, not just a military one.

### "Their intentions have been clear over a sustained period of time." [0:17:10]

China has blockaded the Taiwan Straits seven times in four years. Not a hypothetical. The rehearsals are already happening. Anyone still treating this as a tail risk is not paying attention.

## What Vibe Coding Actually Is

### "Many engineers, they don't look at the code, they don't write code anymore. And that means that you don't need to be an engineer to create software." [0:30:09]

Osika's clearest statement of the shift. The abstraction layer has moved up — **engineering judgement** matters more than syntax now. Relevant to how Loom's own build decisions should be framed going forward.

### "We're seeing people who are first time founders, we're seeing enterprise leaders move much faster together with their teams on this platform." [0:26:30]

Growth is fastest on the enterprise side, not just indie hackers. That's the signal that vibe coding has gone past hobbyist — when enterprise teams are adopting it to **ship faster** alongside their technical staff, the category has crossed the chasm.

## Cost Collapse in Real Numbers

### "it would have cost me $500,000 two years ago. It was built in four hours by an employee" [0:33:37]

The **cost compression** here is staggering — from $500k to under $2k. This is the number to show sceptics. The ROI argument for Loom's own internal tooling is exactly this shape.

### "we're working with some of our customers in pre release to give them access to a co founder that works for you even when you're sleeping" [0:36:29]

The pitch is an agent with access to all your company data, returning each morning with **strategic recommendations** and growth optimisations. Ambitious framing — but it's also the honest direction of travel. Worth watching whether "co-founder" delivers or becomes another chatbot wrapper.

## Replacing SaaS With Custom Builds

### "they've now replaced more than 10 tools that they had, which is bespoke applications. In their case, they're saving more than a million dollars per year." [0:38:44]

One company killed 10 SaaS subscriptions with **custom Lovable builds** and saved over $1M/yr. The Slack-replacement thought experiment Jason floats right before this is the right instinct — the bespoke layer on top of existing APIs is the move, not a cold rebuild.

### "you can continue to use Salesforce, HubSpot and all the tools that you like to use under the hood, but with a bespoke interface on top of it." [0:39:40]

The actual strategy isn't replacement — it's a **custom UI layer** over existing integrations. Smarter than "kill your CRM." Lower switching risk, faster to ship, and you keep the data you already have.

## Judgment Is the New Bottleneck

### "since the engineering is less of the bottleneck, it's more the question of what is the right thing to build." [0:45:50]

The constraint has **shifted from execution to judgment**. This is the cleanest line in the excerpt. Running parallel competing builds inside one org — the CERN coopetition model — is a genuinely interesting implication of that shift.

### "on figuring what to build, figuring out what are the right strategic directions or experiments you should run to improve the outcomes for your business, that's not changing as fast." [0:48:27]

Even with better models, the **strategic layer stays human** — plugging in the right data, asking the right questions, making product calls. Sobering counterweight to the "AI co-founder" pitch two minutes earlier in the same conversation.
