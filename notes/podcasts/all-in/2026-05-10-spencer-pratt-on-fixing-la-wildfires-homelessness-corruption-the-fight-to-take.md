# Notes: Spencer Pratt on Fixing LA: Wildfires, Homelessness, Corruption & the Fight to Take It Back

## Summary

Spencer Pratt — reality TV personality turned LA
mayoral candidate — makes a **credible, fact-dense
case** that LA's collapse is not accidental.

His house burned in the Palisades fire (Jan 7).
A reservoir next door had been **drained six months
earlier**. No sirens. No air support. The mayor was
in Africa. Her deputy was on house arrest.

Pratt's diagnosis: a **corrupt NGO ecosystem**
laundering homeless billions, tribal politics
blocking accountability, and zero law enforcement.

His fix is deliberately un-glamorous:
**enforce existing laws**, audit everything, bring
in private-sector operators, and let commerce
return naturally. He draws on SF Mayor Lurie's
turnaround as the proof-of-concept.

Whether he wins or not, the structural critique
he's making — spend doubles, outcomes halve,
nobody's arrested — is **the correct frame** for
understanding urban Democratic machine failure.

## Quotes

### "I didn't even know that there was this crazy wind weather event."

The city's own emergency communication was so
poor that a phone-addicted local **didn't know a
dangerous weather event was imminent**.
That's the baseline failure — before the empty
reservoir, before the absent mayor.

Pre-disaster warning is a solved problem.
Amber alerts exist. This was a **choice** not to
communicate, not a capability gap.

---

### "I live next to the one you don't know existed, The Palisades Reservoir. 5 million gallons next door to my house."

This wasn't a rumoured asset — it was a **working
drill site**. Fire departments regularly connected
to it. Pratt was inconvenienced by the drills and
considered it reassuring.

The mayor denied its existence in a debate.
That's not spin — that's a **factual claim about
infrastructure she oversees**. One of them is lying
and CBS subsequently sided with Pratt.

---

### "She was in Africa. And you know who was supposed to do it? Her deputy mayor. But he was on house arrest."

During the initial fire response, **LA City never
called in fixed air wing support** to drop water.
The chain of command had collapsed: absent mayor,
incapacitated deputy.

This is the single most damning operational fact
in the episode. Not ideology — just **nobody was
at the controls** during the most destructive urban
fire in modern LA history.

---

### "They just drive around and they go 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6. They're not going in under these encampments and bridges and bushes and Unzipping these tents and going into the sewers."

The **official homeless count methodology is
theatre**. Even the Rand Corporation says the real
number is 30% higher than reported.

When the **measurement is corrupt**, every policy
built on it is also corrupt. "Down 17%" is a
number generated by the same people whose jobs
depend on the problem not being solved.

---

### "Fire aid, 100 million raised. Every single person I talked to messaging me, no one's getting this money. No one's seeing a dollar."

After pushing for investigation, FireAid's own
legal defence letter admitted only **"several"**
NGOs out of 200+ gave directly to fire victims.

"Several" is doing a lot of work in that sentence.
This is the NGO corruption argument made **concrete
and verifiable** — not a theory, a legal filing.

---

### "In the state of California, with the home key rules, the state won't give the city a lot of the money if you require the people to be off of drugs."

State funding rules **actively penalise** housing
programmes that require sobriety. The incentive
structure is not broken by accident.

This is the most structurally important point in
the whole episode. The system **rewards** keeping
people addicted. Any politician who claims to want
to fix homelessness without addressing this rule
is performing, not governing.

---

### "Once you start putting handcuffs on people, watch how many people leave."

A former police chief's line that Pratt uses to
pre-empt the "but where will they go?" objection
to enforcement.

San Francisco under Lurie is the live experiment.
Car break-ins down **87%** — just by enforcing
laws that already existed. The intervention
required was **will**, not legislation.

---

### "I'm never the most experienced person in any of the rooms of my company, but everyone in my company is the most experienced person in what they need to do in that role."

This is the line Pratt was given by successful
CEOs to use against the **"no experience"** attack.
It's a reasonable reframe of what executive
leadership actually is.

The counter-example he uses is sharp: the LADWP
CEO who drained two reservoirs before fire season
was paid **$750,000 a year**. Experience
and competence are not the same thing.

---

### "I don't need to invent anything. I don't need to come up with this utopia of how a city works. You make a city safe and people will put money into it."

The whole platform compressed into four sentences.
**Law enforcement → safety → investment → commerce**
→ tax revenue → fund everything else.

It's boring. That's the point. The city's actual
problems aren't complex — they require **execution**,
not innovation. Pratt is right to resist the
temptation to dress this up.

---

### "They let seven people die in the street every day with our billions of tax dollars, and they say, we need new beds."

His argument: **90% are drug addicts**. Beds without
mandatory treatment don't solve addiction. The
sequencing — treatment first, then housing — is
the thing the NGO model structurally resists.

"New beds" is also a **procurement event**, which
means another NGO contract, which means more
laundered money. The policy and the graft are
the same mechanism.

---

### "When I'm mayor, my plan is, first three weeks, signs up across the city, no more nakedness, no more drug use, no more robbing."

A **signalled transition period** before enforcement
begins — so people self-select out before handcuffs
appear. Tactically sensible; legally cleaner than
an overnight sweep.

This is one of the more thought-through operational
details in the episode. The **warning-then-enforce**
sequence is how Broken Windows actually works when
done well, not as a bludgeon.

## Key Arguments

1. **The Palisades fire was preventable** — drained
   reservoirs, no air support called, absent mayor,
   incapacitated deputy. Not a climate event; an
   **accountability failure** with named individuals.

2. **The NGO homeless-industrial complex is a
   self-sustaining graft machine** — structured to
   maximise spend, minimise outcomes, and punish
   sobriety requirements via state funding rules.
   The corruption is **systemic, not incidental**.

3. **Law enforcement is sufficient** — existing
   laws, if enforced, would clear streets, return
   commerce, and attract private investment without
   new policy. SF's 87% drop in car crime is the
   proof-of-concept.

4. **The "no experience" attack is a deflection** —
   the experienced incumbents drained the reservoir,
   cooked the homeless counts, and flew to Africa.
   **Competence ≠ credentials**; Pratt's play is to
   hire the competent and set the direction.

5. **Tribal politics is the enabler** — voters back
   Bass and Rahman not from conviction but from
   **team identity**. Pratt's pitch is explicitly
   post-partisan: moms, taxpayers, and animal
   lovers cross every demographic line.
