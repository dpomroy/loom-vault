# Notes: Inside the Private Stock Market Boom: SpaceX, Anthropic, OpenAI & the Rise of Secondaries

## Summary

**Private secondary markets** have hit record
volume — double the 2021 peak — and are now
a genuine third exit path alongside IPOs
and M&A.

The core tension: companies stay private
longer (founder preference, abundant capital)
but that creates **liquidity starvation** for
employees and VCs. Secondaries solve this,
but at a cost — retail investors risk being
exit liquidity at peak valuations.

The Forge-Schwab deal is the structural
signal: private equity is becoming a
**retail asset class**, whether the market
is ready or not. Smart operators are selling
into the frenzy; smart retail should be
buying earlier-stage names, not SpaceX at
$1.75T with 14 levered ETFs launching on
IPO day.

## Quotes

### "Secondaries are now competing with IPOs and acquisitions as the principal way that these guys are exiting." [0:00:52]

The secondary market is now a **primary exit mechanism** — not a niche workaround. That's a structural shift, not a cycle. For anyone building infrastructure around private markets (Loom-adjacent thinking), this is the signal that the asset class has arrived.

### "The secondary market is at record volume." [0:00:52]

Record volume, and not just relative to recent years — **double the 2021 peak**, which at the time felt extreme. The chart context here matters: more money flowing in than out of VC for five consecutive years, and secondaries are the release valve.

### "There are a lot of people who are very, very wealthy on paper, but actually cash poor." [0:03:38]

Ten million on paper, no house. Year **seven, eight, nine** at a company you believe in — and you can't buy anything real. This is the human cost of staying private. Secondaries aren't just financial plumbing; they're retention and morale infrastructure.

### "I don't think there is actually a good reason to stay private longer." [0:04:38]

Notably, **all three panelists agree** — the bears on going public aren't on this stage. The counterpoint (from Kelly) is that the capital environment now *allows* extended private life, not that it's optimal. Founders choose it for comfort, not strategic advantage.

### "When you're private, you do not get clean information as the CEO and the management team, because people want access." [0:08:20]

Ask hard questions, **lose your seat at the table**. The sycophantic dynamic is structural, not personal. Zuckerberg's HTML5 mistake is the canonical example — private investors sold him a story because they needed the next round. Public markets fix this. Most founders don't know what they're missing.

### "Had I been getting rigorous, detailed questions from really smart public equity investors, I think I would have made the second" [0:09:03]

Zuckerberg's own conclusion: **public scrutiny would have caught the HTML5 bet faster**. This is the underrated case for going public early. The discipline isn't a burden — it's signal. Private CEOs are operating on filtered, self-serving information.

### "We are selling into this." [0:16:34]

Brad says it plainly — LPs want **DPI**, and at 4-5x with high prices, you sell. VCs only think about buying; the sell discipline is borrowed from public markets. The secondary market is what makes that sell decision executable without burning founder relationships.

### "There are 14 ETFs launching on the day of the SpaceX IPO that are levered ETFs into SpaceX at like whatever, 1.75 trillion." [0:29:53]

14 **levered ETFs** on IPO day is a contrary indicator, not a buying signal. "We may not be at the top, but we ain't at the bottom." The product-launch frenzy is the tell. When the derivative product arrives before price discovery has settled, retail is the exit, not the opportunity.

### "The retail investor coming into this space needs to look down market and look at interesting opportunities that aren't the things that are on CNBC every day." [0:27:33]

SpaceX at $30B in 2018 was the trade. **SpaceX at $1.75T is a different trade entirely.** Kelly's actual pitch to retail is: use the infrastructure to get in *early*, not to finally touch the names you've been hearing about for a decade. That's the right framing — and almost nobody is saying it clearly enough.

### "99 was Vegas on a Friday night after way too many drugs." [0:31:02]

Brad's point: the **current market is not 1999**. CMGI had no revenue, went from $2 to $2,000 in six months. Anthropic and SpaceX are real businesses with real revenue. A 2021-style valuation excess is the correct comp — not dot-com. That changes your drawdown expectations significantly.

### "I think they're engaging at some level... they're chasing gambling terms." [0:23:22]

VCs without **exposure to a trillion-dollar name** are writing speculative call options on neo-labs to maintain a credible story. Gavin's read: franchise risk is making mid-tier VCs do irrational things. The performance dispersion in this cycle will be brutal — concentrated at the top, mediocre everywhere else.

### "Keller had the idea of, we're going to go to African countries and we are going to help or deliver medicines to these small villages." [0:38:24]

Zipline's real moat: **seven years of real-world autonomous flight data** gathered delivering blood and medicine in Africa — cutting maternal mortality by 90-95% in some countries. The regulatory arbitrage was the insight. You couldn't get FAA approval; you could get Rwanda's. Now that data advantage comes to the US market. Classic under-the-radar compounding.

## Key Arguments

1. **Secondary markets are structural, not cyclical.**
   Volume is double the 2021 peak; secondaries
   now rival IPOs as exit mechanisms. The
   Forge-Schwab deal institutionalises this
   as a retail asset class.

2. **Staying private longer has no good
   strategic rationale** — it's a founder
   comfort choice that starves employees of
   liquidity and insulates CEOs from honest
   feedback. Public market discipline is
   *underrated*, not burdensome.

3. **Retail risk is real at current valuations.**
   Smart money (Brad, Gavin) is explicitly
   selling into secondary demand. Retail
   buyers at peak prices with levered products
   are the exit liquidity, not the
   beneficiaries.

4. **Performance in this VC cycle will be
   extremely concentrated.** Firms without
   a trillion-dollar name in their portfolio
   face franchise risk and are making
   increasingly irrational bets to compensate.

5. **The right retail play is earlier-stage
   secondaries**, not the headline names
   already on CNBC. The SpaceX-at-$30B trade
   is gone; the next version of that trade
   exists in less-covered names — Revolut,
   ARIA, Neurorobotics, Zipline.
