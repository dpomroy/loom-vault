# Notes: Senators John Fetterman and Dave McCormick: Bipartisanship, Money in DC, Datacenters, Graham Platner

## Summary

Two Pennsylvania senators — Democrat Fetterman,
Republican McCormick — argue that **bipartisan
cooperation** is both possible and necessary,
using PA as a working proof-of-concept.

Core thesis: the AI + energy boom is creating a
**blue-collar renaissance** in PA, but it's
being sabotaged by dark money misinformation,
some of it likely CCP-linked. Both senators back
data centres, oppose a moratorium, and reject
their own parties' extremes.

The filibuster section is a rare moment of a
politician **admitting he was wrong** publicly.
The money-in-politics closer lands hard: $500M
spent on one Senate race, mostly on destruction.
Useful lens for thinking about institutional
trust collapse and who actually shapes policy.

## Quotes

### "If you allow Iran to become nuclear, you know, that's going to seem quaint, you know, if $5 a gallon" [0:07:34]

Fetterman's framing here is sharp — he's reordering the cost-benefit, not denying the pain of energy prices. Most politicians dodge the trade-off entirely. He names it. Whether you agree or not, this is what honest reasoning in public sounds like.

### "The entire Democratic Party, including myself, we were so wrong about the filibuster." [0:10:24]

Fetterman credits Manchin and Sinema — the two Democrats who blocked filibuster elimination — saying history has vindicated them. A sitting senator saying "we were completely wrong" on a flagship policy position is genuinely rare. File this under: institutions matter more than your current team's convenience.

### "We are gonna lose capitalism unless we find a combination of ways to address it." [0:14:39]

McCormick frames himself as a capitalism beneficiary who believes the system itself is now at risk from wealth concentration. That's a more sophisticated warning than either left or right usually delivers. The question he can't answer is *what specifically* fixes it — the Invest America accounts idea that follows is thin.

### "There's enormous misinformation and I think that misinformation is largely being driven by China and outside forces." [0:30:29]

McCormick draws a line between deliberate foreign-driven disinfo and genuine local misunderstanding, arguing both need different responses. This is relevant to the horse racing/trading context too — when you see coordinated narrative shifts, ask who benefits. The CCP benefiting from a US data centre moratorium is not a conspiracy theory.

### "We had $92 billion of investment committed." [0:28:45]

McCormick's PA energy and innovation summit in July drew energy CEOs, AI CEOs, Fetterman, Governor Shapiro, the President, and the Cabinet. $92B committed in one summit is a concrete signal, not rhetoric. This is the AI infrastructure buildout happening at state level, not just Silicon Valley boardrooms.

### "The most secure job in Pennsylvania right now is a seasoned welder." [0:37:22]

McCormick and Fetterman both agree: electricians and welders in their late teens and early 20s are making over $100k a year on data centre and energy sites, and employers can't hire fast enough. This is the actual blue-collar AI dividend — not chatbots, but the construction wave underneath them. Worth tracking as a counter-narrative to the "AI destroys jobs" frame.

### "Now my party is driving for a moratorium for data centers. Do you think China doesn't love that?" [0:35:08]

Fetterman says America is six to eight months ahead of China in AI, making a Democratic-led moratorium on data centres a strategic gift to Beijing. Blunt framing. He's essentially calling parts of his own party useful idiots. Whether the 6-8 month lead figure is accurate is worth stress-testing.

### "It's not a seat worth trying to maintain if I'm going to lie or to pretend that things aren't absolutely true." [0:25:35]

Fetterman acknowledges he can be primaried, but says he'd rather lose the seat than vote against his actual beliefs — specifically on Israel and opposing government shutdowns. Easy to say. Harder to mean. His voting record at 93% Democrat alignment suggests he's not actually a centrist — he's a loyalist with a few high-profile defections. The maverick framing is slightly oversold.

### "Collectively, $300 million were spent to destroy our reputations. Think what $300 million could do for Pennsylvania." [0:42:17]

Fetterman's point: the money spent on negative campaigning against both of them could have gone directly into the state instead. The strongest line in the episode. No policy proposal attached, but as an indictment of campaign finance dysfunction it hits harder than anything else said in 45 minutes.

### "We have the far left and the far right converging on an anti" [0:40:25]

McCormick notes that opposition to data centres isn't just a Democrat problem — the far right America First movement has landed in the same place as the far left, creating a horseshoe coalition against AI infrastructure. Classic horseshoe theory in a live policy context. When your opposition includes both MAGA and DSA, you're probably doing something economically sensible and politically complicated.

## Key Arguments

1. **Bipartisan cooperation is structurally forced
   in Pennsylvania** — the state's political
   geography (urban Dem, rural Republican) means
   you can't win without a cross-coalition, so
   both senators have a genuine incentive to
   collaborate that most senators lack.

2. **Data centres = blue-collar boom**, not a
   tech-elite story — construction jobs,
   electricians, welders, logistics, restaurants
   all benefit; the economic case is empirical
   but being drowned out by misinformation.

3. **The data centre opposition is partly
   foreign-engineered** — McCormick and Fetterman
   both argue CCP-linked money is funding
   anti-AI-infrastructure narratives in the US,
   echoing the playbook used against fracking.

4. **Both parties are drifting toward extremes**
   that make governance impossible — Dems toward
   socialism and anti-Israel positions,
   Republicans toward figures like Graham Platner;
   the senators represent a shrinking centre that
   actually wins swing states.

5. **Campaign finance has broken the system** —
   a $500M Senate race, mostly spent on
   destruction, is now considered *modest*;
   without reform, the incentive structure pushes
   candidates toward rage-bait over policy.
