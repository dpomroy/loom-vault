# Notes: More Trillion Dollar IPOs, Anthropic $3T, Zuck's Price War, China Ends Open Source?, Trump Accounts

## Summary

The episode's central claim is that **Anthropic will IPO before OpenAI**, driven by a simpler corporate structure and rumoured revenues north of $100B. SpaceX is the structural blueprint both labs will use for pricing and lockup mechanics. The hosts treat this as near-certain, not speculative.

Underneath the IPO optimism sits a harder question. Token costs are doubling every 45 days, yet measurable productivity gains have plateaued around 5%. Enterprise AI spend is compounding fast, but the CFO conversation — "show me the return" — hasn't happened yet. When it does, the hosts expect a reckoning.

The open-versus-closed debate gets reframed as a **maturity cycle**, not a permanent split. Frontier models are for discovering new workflows; once a use case stabilises, open models become the rational choice. China restricting its own open-source models mirrors exactly what OpenAI did early on — stay open until you're near the frontier, then close to capture value. Meanwhile, sovereign AI strategies mean every country wants a stack it controls, which caps the TAM of closed US models.

The second half pivots to Trump Accounts — tax-advantaged S&P 500 accounts seeded at birth. The $1,000 government contribution is almost beside the point. The real insight is the **compounding window**: years 0–25 are the highest-leverage period, and no tax-advantaged vehicle existed before earned income until now. With employer matches and a Roth IRA rollover, a child starting at birth could reach $10M by retirement on minimal additional input.

The political pitch rests on ownership: an account you control and can pass to heirs behaves differently — psychologically and financially — than an entitlement. That distinction, the hosts argue, is what makes this durable across administrations.

## Trump Accounts: Structure and Mechanics

### "the antidote to more socialism is more capitalism" [1:13:27]

The clearest ideological thesis of the segment — and Brad delivers it as a bumper sticker, not an argument. Whether you buy the framing or not, it's the engine driving the whole programme.

The political positioning is deliberate. Making every kid a shareholder is a structural counter to populist redistribution — harder to argue with than a tax cut.

### "you can donate up to $5,000 a year to your kid as long as they're under 18. And it's not just you, it's any friends and family or others can contribute as well, which is new." [1:17:30]

Tax-free compounding until 18, employer contributions of up to $2,500 also tax-free — this is the mechanic that CPAs are excited about, and it's genuinely novel.

Sacks is right that the free-money angle overshadows the tax angle in media coverage. For anyone with kids, this is closer to "obvious move" territory than political statement.

### "if you start with 2 to $300,000 at age 18, you'll be at $10 million plus by age 60 if you just let it compound" [1:22:26]

Assumes standard historical market returns — not guaranteed, but not a crazy assumption either over a 42-year horizon.

The number sounds ridiculous until you run it. 7% real returns on $250k over 42 years is actually ~$4M, so $10M is bullish. But the directional point stands — compounding from birth is genuinely powerful.

## Why Ownership Beats Entitlement

### "if a kid doesn't have any money, it's hard to get excited about learning about money. But if a kid's in the game and has 12,000 bucks in the seventh grade — now you got my attention." [1:30:13]

This is the most underrated argument in the whole segment. Financial literacy education is notoriously ineffective in the abstract — skin in the game changes the incentive to actually learn.

Behavioural angle is solid. You don't learn to care about compounding by reading about it.

Low bar historically, but the team (Tenev, Gebbia, Dell) is credible. Worth watching whether the UX holds at 50M+ accounts.

Governor Moore of Maryland's framing — and it's the most politically useful quote in the segment because it comes from a Democrat.

The bipartisan cover matters for longevity. Programmes that get named after one president tend to get unwound by the next. The "Invest America Act" branding is the insurance policy here.

## The Compounding Window Insight

The whole account framing hangs on this line.

### "The size of the hill in this country, we've cut off the first third of the hill forever." [1:40:46]

Simple, sticky framing — the kind that
