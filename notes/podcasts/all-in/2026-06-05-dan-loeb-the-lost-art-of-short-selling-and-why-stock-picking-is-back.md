# Notes: Dan Loeb: The Lost Art of Short Selling, and Why Stock Picking is Back

## Summary

Dan Loeb (Third Point, ~$30B AUM) argues that
**stock picking and short selling are back**
after years of being crowded out.

His core evolution:
- Started event-driven (spinoffs, bankruptcies,
  distressed debt)
- Shifted toward **quality + moat + management**
  as tech became unavoidable
- Now runs a multi-strat platform: hedge fund,
  private credit, CLO, insurance

Key conviction: **Nvidia is undervalued** on a
2-3 year earnings basis. Homebuilders are a
strong short — structural, not just rate-driven.

He also worked to secure **Ross Ulbricht's
pardon**, framing it as criminal justice reform
(disproportionate sentencing), not crypto
politics. Philanthropy thread: education reform
and income inequality as the same root problem.

## Quotes

### "The lost art of short selling has come back and it's absolutely critical." [0:00:06]

The framing matters — "lost art" implies it was
structural, not cyclical. A decade of QE
suppressed dispersion; now that rates are real,
bad businesses actually fall. Short selling is
an alpha signal again, not just a hedge.

### "I was like a Chinese corporation that was copying and reverse engineering and taking everything in and creating my database of knowledge and my own operating system." [0:06:43]

Loeb watched Tepper and others closely —
not as a student, but as a collector, extracting
the best of each. This is a better model
than finding one mentor. Pattern synthesis beats
apprenticeship.

### "You could be technologically illiterate or just say, I don't do it. And you could also be even more or less, you know, up until the GFC, I think you could be more or less economically illiterate and make a lot of money." [0:09:10]

The implication is blunt: two full categories
of ignorance were viable strategies. Neither
is now. This is a useful benchmark for how much
the market's complexity floor has risen since
2008.

### "Activism without proxy contests is like Catholicism without hell." [0:00:14]

Loeb in one line: the threat has to be
credible, or no one moves. This is the
oldest principle in negotiation — leverage only
works if the other side believes you'll use it.
Dry enough to be quotable forever.

### "I've seen too many people get run over by shorts that have dumb valuations, but they get captured on Reddit or one of these other things." [0:16:32]

Pure valuation shorts are a trap — the market
can stay irrational longer than you stay
solvent, especially with retail coordination
now. Loeb's short framework needs a
**catalyst**, not just a number. Worth
remembering before any value-based short thesis.

### "The home building industry was first structurally impaired because of the way that they were all pretending to be NVR, which is they all pretending to be asset light, but they had massive commitments to these land pools." [0:18:25]

The short thesis here is accounting opacity —
calling option-like commitments "options" when
they're really fixed capital obligations.
Classic Loeb: event-driven lens applied to a
structural balance-sheet distortion. Not just a
rate play.

### "I think we'll look back at some point in time and say that was a foolish way to think about Nvidia given its dominant position and its valuation relative." [0:21:18]

The argument: people are anchoring on absolute
market cap ($5T feels like a ceiling) rather
than earnings multiples and competitive
moat. He's making the same argument that
was right about Google and Amazon. Could be
right again — or this time the ceiling is real.

### "Nvidia feels like a safe short. By the way, Google was a safe short. Amazon was a safe short." [0:22:14]

The structural reason: long/short pods
**need** to be short something, so dominant
large-caps become the path of least resistance
for the short book. That mechanical pressure
suppresses price regardless of fundamentals.
Useful when evaluating why a quality stock
seems "stuck."

### "The problem is that the unions and the basic principles that we all use in business, which is accountability and merit and cultivating talent, is set aside for the benefit of adults who are part of these unions." [0:24:30]

Loeb's education thesis: the failure is
**structural**, not financial. More money
into a broken incentive system produces the
same output. This is the charter school
argument in one sentence — and it's genuinely
hard to rebut on the merits.

### "There's no recourse through the system to get someone with a life sentence out of jail. This will only work with a presidential pardon." [0:30:18]

The system has no self-correction mechanism
for its worst outcomes. Loeb's Ulbricht
work is interesting because it was explicitly
**outside the system** — not legal advocacy but
political leverage. That's honest about how
power actually works.

## Key Arguments

1. **Short selling is back as a real discipline.**
   The zero-rate era suppressed it. Now
   dispersion is real, but pure valuation shorts
   are still dangerous — you need a structural or
   event-driven catalyst, not just a bad
   multiple.

2. **Tech literacy is now non-negotiable** for
   any capital allocator. Pre-GFC you could
   ignore macro; pre-AI you could ignore tech.
   Neither is true anymore. Every asset class
   is correlated to the tech through-line.

3. **Moat assessment has shifted from product
   to management.** Predicting which technology
   wins 10 years out is near-impossible (see:
   IBM, AOL, Yahoo). The better bet is finding
   **adaptable management teams** with proven
   track records of staying ahead of disruption.

4. **Nvidia is undervalued** on a 2-3 year
   earnings basis. The market is anchoring on
   absolute size rather than relative earnings
   growth and competitive dominance. The same
   mistake was made with Amazon and Google.

5. **Income inequality's root cause is
   education failure**, not wealth concentration.
   The system is broken by design — union
   incentives protect adults, not children. More
   funding doesn't fix misaligned accountability
   structures.
