# Notes: Bill Maris: How Google Could Crush AI Competitors, Why Small Funds Win, and AI's Atari Stage

## Summary

Bill Maris — founder of Google Ventures,
now running Section32 — argues that **small
VC funds structurally outperform large ones**,
and that AI is still at its "Atari stage."

Key bets:

- Funds under **$750M** return 4.76x vs 2.42x
  for funds over $1B
- Google could **crush OpenAI/Anthropic** by
  slashing token prices — the business model
  risk is underpriced
- AI's real gains will come from **infrastructure**
  (physics engines, controllers, platforms),
  not bigger models
- VC incentives are **structurally broken** —
  large funds reward mediocrity at the GP level

Matters because it's a clean-eyed framework
for where value actually accrues in the AI
cycle — and a warning about who ends up
holding the bag.

## Key Arguments

1. **Small funds structurally outperform.**
   Sub-$750M funds return nearly 2x the
   multiple of funds over $1B, and represent
   95% of top-decile performers. This is
   math, not style preference.

2. **Google can kill the AI price war.**
   Token costs are the lever. If Google
   decides to discount Gemini aggressively,
   OpenAI and Anthropic face an existential
   margin squeeze with no obvious defence.

3. **AI is infrastructure-stage, not
   product-stage.** Current AI looks like
   Atari: brittle, turn-based, no memory.
   The bet isn't bigger models — it's the
   physics engines, controllers, and
   platforms that make AI actually work.

4. **VC incentives are broken top-to-bottom.**
   LPs need size for allocation reasons.
   GPs earn more on fees from a 1x large
   fund than carried interest on a 3x small
   one. Entrepreneurs take inflated valuations.
   All three parties rationally perpetuate
   a bad system.

5. **Late-stage overhang is a retail risk.**
   Companies staying private longer means
   401k holders buy at peak valuations via
   passive funds. The "benefit of humanity"
   framing masks that ordinary investors
   are being positioned as the exit.
