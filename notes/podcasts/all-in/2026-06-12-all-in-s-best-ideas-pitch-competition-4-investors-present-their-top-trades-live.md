# Notes: All-In's Best Ideas Pitch Competition: 4 Investors Present Their Top Trades Live

## Summary

The core thesis of this episode is simple: **mispriced hard assets and structural moats** are the best place to put capital right now.

Jake Dreyfus opens with Talen Energy, a nuclear power company trading 45% below what it would cost to rebuild. His argument is that power scarcity is already locked in — 20 years of underinvestment did that — and AI demand just accelerates a timeline that was already tight. The trade is the classic Sam Zell move: buy the hard asset cheap and wait for the market to catch up.

Aaron Cohen pitches MGM as a stock the market is systematically mis-valuing. It has bought back half its float in six years. It has a Japan casino license coming around 2030 that history suggests the market will start pricing in roughly now. Barry Diller's takeover attempt, Cohen argues, is simply an outsider trying to snatch the Japanese optionality before it gets priced in.

The two smaller pitches both turn on **moat depth**. Actis Oncology is a radiopharmaceutical play where the moat is physical: actinium isotopes are inaccessible to China and generics don't operate in this space. The pitch explicitly warns against falling in love with the science — the attraction is the risk-reward structure. Geodnet is a decentralised GPS network that achieved 100x location accuracy over standard GPS and outbuilt 30 years of incumbent capex in four years. Eighty percent of revenue goes to buying back its own token, making it behave more like a yield instrument than a speculative bet.

The audience voted for Talen; the host panel picked MGM as the upset winner.

## Power Scarcity as Structural Thesis

### "We do not need AI demand to keep the power markets incredibly tight for the next 20 years. AI demand just turbocharges. That's all it does." [0:17:52]

The cleanest line in the whole excerpt. Dreyfus is separating the structural thesis from the hype — power scarcity was already the trade. AI is a multiplier, not the premise.

The discipline here matters: it makes the thesis much harder to dismiss as AI-bubble-dependent.

### "If you believe that artificial intelligence is going to be helpful for national security and military affairs, then you either have it or you're dead." [0:19:56]

Dreyfus escalates his argument in three steps — scientific breakthroughs, robotics, then national security. The third one lands hardest because it reframes power infrastructure as a **sovereignty** issue, not just an investment one.

This is the kind of framing that makes policy makers move — and policy is what unblocks the permitting.

### "With a data center, you put electricity in, and on the other end, instead of gasoline or jet fuel, out comes photons or tokens or intelligence, whatever you want to call it, but it's the same thing." [0:20:32]

The data centre-as-refinery analogy is genuinely useful. It maps a familiar commodity-world mental model onto AI infrastructure — makes the **capex intensity** and input dependency obvious.

Useful framing to steal when explaining why power is the real AI bottleneck.

## Talen Energy: Buy Below Replacement Cost

### "Rarely have I ever seen a company in six years buy half their float back." [0:03:52]

Cowen buries the lead slightly — this is actually the strongest signal in his MGM pitch. Buybacks at that scale combined with an activist buyer at 26% is a **structural squeeze** on supply, regardless of the Japan/Dubai optionality.

### "Talon is maybe 3x upside, 5x upside, but it's eight years out." [1:03:25]

The holding period kills it for most. Add **interest rate sensitivity** through power purchase agreement discounting and the 15x headline number compresses fast if rates stay elevated. Good pitch, wrong macro moment.

## MGM: Japan and the Unpriced Option

### "What he's really doing is now trying to pick off the company to get the Japanese opportunity, which we think is worth will more than double the stock." [0:08:45]

Cowen's read on Diller is the most interesting interpretive move in the MGM pitch. If correct, the $48 bid is an **opening offer**, not fair value — and the market is pricing it as a takeout rather than a floor.

### "The market started caring about it about three years before it opened. So the answer is they should care about it. The reality is it tends to be about three years before it opens. Well, we're almost in that time frame." [0:12:03]

Cowen uses Macau as a timing **precedent** for when the Osaka option gets priced in. That's a reasonable data point — the market does tend to re-rate on visibility horizon, not opening date.

Worth noting: this logic cuts both ways — if the catalyst is already known, you need a reason it's still mispriced.

### "they're making a million bucks a day in incremental ebitda every day that they have a show at the Sphere at the Venetian Hotel, which is an unbelievable statistic which tells you that when you have the entertainment draw, the gambling revenue just flies." [1:01:34]

A single data point that reframes the MGM thesis entirely. The bull case isn't just gaming — it's entertainment as a **demand multiplier** on the underlying asset.

## Why Biotech Is Usually a Bad Bet

### "Generally speaking, investing in biotech companies is a horrible idea sandwiched somewhere between movies, wineries and spacs." [0:27:51]

The self-aware opener is doing real work — it pre-empts the obvious objection and signals the pitcher knows the base rate. The follow-up reframe ("poker players… focus on margin of safety") is the actual thesis.

### "We don't want to fall in love with the science. We fall in love with the risk reward." [0:27:51]

Probably the most transferable idea in the whole pitch. Applies directly to any domain where technical enthusiasm crowds out sober probability-weighted returns — horse racing included.

## Actis Oncology: The Radiopharma Moat

### "Modern day radiopharmaceuticals, like a swarm of microdrones small enough to navigate the bloodstream and find their target by molecular recognition. Then detonate a precisely sized warhead with a blast radius of 100 microns or the diameter of a single cell." [0:27:51]

The military framing isn't just theatre — it maps meaningfully onto actual mechanism-of-action differences between modalities. 100-micron blast radius vs. full-body chemo is a genuinely useful mental model for understanding why specificity matters.

### "Generics generally don't traffic in radiopharma, and because the class involves radioisotopes, it's off limits to China." [0:27:51]

This is the moat argument in two sentences. The actinium supply chain is a Cold War artefact — US Radium-233 waste — so the barrier isn't IP, it's physical supply. Hard to replicate, hard to route around.

### "I think the China risk is legit because I've seen it across the board in biotech, everything gets ripped off and people go to China, but they could have a hit and Lilly could bid on it in six months if they actually get a good readout." [1:03:25]

Classic binary biotech payoff structure — 50-75% downside on a bad readout or IP theft, acquisition premium on a good one. The **acquisition optionality** is real but it doesn't change the lottery ticket nature of the bet.

## Biotech Platforms vs Point Solutions

### "if you ever get a biotech company that can become a platform and they have a mechanism, whether it's of drugging, whether it's targeting, if you have something that is broadly applicable, that is when you can get these really really big hundred billion dollar plus outcomes in biotech which are rare." [0:59:28]

The platform-vs-one-trick framing is the right lens for early biotech. The rare part is doing the work — most drugs are point solutions. Actis gets credit for having a credible platform story.

## Geodnet: Decentralised GPS Infrastructure

### "Geodnet fundamentally is a new uses a technology called RTK or real time kinematics where you can localize your location down to about 2 centimeters for context, GPS, roughly the precision is about 2 meters. So you're getting about 100x accuracy for very precise geolocation." [0:40:19]

The core product claim in plain numbers. 2cm vs 2m is the entire value proposition — everything else (drones, ag robots, AV mapping) is just a list of who needs that gap closed.

### "Today the Geo Net network is taking of that 11 million in revenue. Roughly 80% of it is being used to make open market purchases of Geo tokens." [0:40:19]

This is what separates Geodnet from most DePIN failures: it's a real buyback, on-chain and verifiable, not a promise. The other 20% covers all R&D and sales — the unit economics are unusually tight for this stage.

### "I feel about crypto exactly the way I do about snowboarding." [1:00:14]

Honest self-awareness from a 25-year equities investor. Knowing where your edge ends is underrated discipline — most people expand into new asset classes and just lose money with extra steps.

### "It's going to replace all RTK and all GPS in the next decade. It's an inevitable piggyback on systems that are already going up." [1:03:25]

Boldest claim of the episode. "Inevitable" is doing a lot of heavy lifting here — the judges mostly ranked Geodnet last, which suggests conviction wasn't contagious. Worth watching, not buying on a thesis alone.
