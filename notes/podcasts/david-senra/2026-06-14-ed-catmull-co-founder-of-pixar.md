# Notes: Ed Catmull, Co-founder of Pixar

## Summary

Ed Catmull's central belief is that **honest feedback** is the rarest thing in any organisation.

Most companies claim to want the truth. Catmull's argument is that they are structurally designed to prevent it — politics and deference to authority push groups toward what the leader wants to hear, not what is real. His fix was the **Braintrust**: a room where anyone could challenge a film's direction, but nobody could order a change. Persuasion only. That one structural rule changed everything.

Steve Jobs understood this instinctively. He knew his voice was so powerful it could distort the room, so he held back. Catmull's own job in those meetings wasn't to make creative decisions — it was to watch the dynamics and protect the conditions that let good decisions emerge.

Disney had full access to Pixar's methods and still couldn't replicate them. Catmull's diagnosis: they assumed the secret lived in three specific people, not in a process. That shallow reading made copying feel impossible, so they never truly tried.

The commercial philosophy follows from the creative one. "Quality is the best business plan" is a direct rejection of short-term optimisation. Catmull points to GE and Boeing as proof of where the opposite approach leads. When a project is struggling, the signal he watches isn't output — it's whether the team is still in it together.

Culture, he warns, breaks silently. New employees don't follow what leadership says; they copy what they see. The gap between stated values and actual behaviour is where problems accumulate, invisibly, until it's too late.

## Why Honest Feedback Is Rare

### "Every company says they do that. Most of them are full of shit." [0:00:09]

The setup for everything else Catmull says about the Braintrust.
Leaders surround themselves with people who reflect back
what they want to hear — and mistake that for **insight**.

### "Getting at underlying factors is inherently a long term strategy and it's a difficult one and it's timely." [0:00:09]

The honest admission most management advice skips.
Fast decisions feel efficient — they're usually just
**shallow**. Peeling layers costs time you often don't want to spend.

### "You go to your first conclusion and you stop thinking." [0:39:22]

The root cause of Disney Animation's decline in one line.
They decided Pixar's edge was Jobs + Lasseter + Catmull
and stopped asking **what** those people were actually doing.
Comfortable attribution is the enemy of real learning.

## Steve Jobs And Room Dynamics

### "Steve had such a powerful voice that it didn't matter when he spoke. He was going to have this extremely strong effect on the dynamics of the room, and he understood that." [0:12:51]

The reason Jobs was banned wasn't that he was wrong —
it's that his presence **warped** the room regardless of content.
Catmull understood that dynamics ARE the product.

### "There was nothing that Steve ever said that had not been said by somebody else before in one of those meetings." [0:15:05]

This is the sharpest observation in the excerpt.
The Braintrust was already surfacing the right answers —
Jobs just had the **authority weight** to make directors actually hear them.

### "Paying attention to the dynamics of those rooms is the job. They're the ones that are making the movies. I'm not making the movie." [0:17:59]

Catmull's clearest statement of what a leader actually does.
Not output — **conditions**. Rare for someone at his level
to say it this plainly and mean it.

## The Braintrust Structure

### "When we're done that we will have created Michael Eisner's Worst Nightmare." [0:23:42]

Jobs' logic for the simultaneous IPO and Toy Story release.
Complete the contract, gain the experience, go public,
then **renegotiate from strength** — or become a direct competitor.
Chess while Eisner was playing checkers.

### "Nobody ever asked, what are you doing? And there are no secrets. We're not hiding anything ever." [0:39:21]

Disney had contractual access to everything Pixar was doing, yet nobody asked why Pixar was succeeding while Disney was declining.
This is a damning indictment of **incurious leadership**.
If your competitor is beating you and you have a front-row seat,
not asking questions is an active choice to fail.

## Keeping A Project Alive

### "If the team is together and they're laughing and then they're angsting and all that together, then that's when you say, okay, we just keep going." [0:42:12]

Catmull's real-time signal for whether to keep pushing a struggling film was the **spirit of the team** — not the quality of the current cut.
Most organisations kill hard projects by judging early drafts
on output quality rather than team cohesion.
Useful heuristic for Loom: is the team *engaged* with the problem,
not just the artefact?

### "If you take on a hard problem and you just keep pushing on it, then the fact that it was hard is what's going to make it different." [0:43:08]

Difficulty isn't a warning sign — it's the mechanism. Easy problems produce derivative work; hard problems force genuine novelty.
This inverts the normal risk calculus completely.
The **problem's resistance** is the quality filter, not a reason
to de-scope.

### "if you don't have a film out there, then you want people to enjoy going to the movies" [1:17:03]

Lucas's competitive logic is structurally
different to most industries. A healthy ecosystem
lifts all boats — your rivals making great work
grows the whole pie, not shrinks yours.

## Quality As Business Strategy

### "Quality is the best business plan." [0:48:52]

Catmull contrasts this with Jack Welch — Forbes' Manager of the Century — whose short-term growth obsession gutted GE's research, fragmented the company, and exported the same destructive principles to Boeing.
The Welch story is the **empirical proof** of what happens when you
optimise for annual growth rate instead of product quality.
Boeing is the most visceral example you can give someone.

### "I do think that if I know that I'm wrong half the time, that I catch it earlier. I spend less time on the wrong decision." [1:09:42]

Catmull's actual edge wasn't a better hit rate — it was a faster correction rate. Holding the prior that you're probably wrong makes you notice contradicting evidence sooner.
This is calibration over confidence.
Most operators confuse **error rate** with error *duration* —
Catmull's insight is that you can't fix the former, only the latter.

## The Limits Of Solo Thinking

### "trying to answer it is an act of separation... I never was" [1:14:08]

Catmull's most important insight in the clip.
The instinct to isolate your personal contribution
is itself the problem — it breaks the mental model
that made the work possible in the first place.

### "It's not how much can I do on my own? It's how much can I do with others?" [1:14:08]

Clean reframe worth keeping.
The guy who left to "test himself without a
safety net" misunderstood what the net was —
it was just good colleagues thinking together.

## Culture Breaks Silently

### "there were three times in the history of Pixar in which a group arose that felt like they were second class and we missed it and we were looking for it" [1:27:52]

This is the uncomfortable admission.
They were actively scanning for this failure mode
and still got caught three times. Most orgs
aren't even scanning.

### "it doesn't matter what you say, they're emulating the behaviors that they see" [1:29:53]

Culture is **observed behaviour**, not stated values.
New hires watched senior people go home at 6pm
and copied it — while those same seniors
complained the new cohort had no energy.
Neither group said a word.

### "if somebody violates a rule of common sense... you don't make a rule so that nobody else makes the same mistake" [1:31:26]

Every new rule is a **permission tax** on everyone
who wasn't the problem. Catmull's instinct is right
and almost universally ignored by scaling orgs.

## Key Arguments

1. **Feedback mechanisms are usually theatre**
   — politics and deference to power make
   groups converge on what the leader wants,
   not what's true.

2. **Decoupling authority from advice**
   is the structural fix — the Braintrust
   worked because no one in the room could
   *order* a change, only persuade.

3. **Managing group dynamics IS the job**
   at the top; Catmull watched the room,
   he didn't make the film.

4. **Shallow attribution killed Disney's
   copy** — assuming the secret lived in
   three people made imitation feel
   impossible, so they never seriously tried.

5. **"Quality is the best business plan"**
   is the direct inverse of Welch-style
   short-term optimisation — GE and Boeing
   are the empirical counter-argument.

6. **Team cohesion, not current output**,
   is the real signal for whether to keep
   a struggling project alive.

7. **Culture transmits through behaviour,
   not policy** — new employees mirror
   what they see; the gap between that
   and what leadership *says* is where
   problems quietly accumulate.
