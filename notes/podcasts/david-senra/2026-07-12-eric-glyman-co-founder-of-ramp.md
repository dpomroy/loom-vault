# Notes: Eric Glyman, Co-founder of Ramp

## Summary

Ramp's founding insight was a deliberate **inversion**: credit cards make money when customers spend more, so Ramp was built to do the opposite — save customers time and money. That single structural choice put Ramp in opposition to the entire incumbent industry from day one.

Glyman's competitive framing is striking. He doesn't compare Ramp to banks or expense-management tools. He compares it to AI labs. Both businesses are selling knowledge-work automation. Ramp's edge is that it sits on the **governance layer** where money actually moves — not just where intelligence is generated.

AI is central to how he thinks about Ramp's future. As intelligence becomes cheap and abundant, more agents will act autonomously in the world. Every autonomous agent eventually needs to make a payment. That means more spend needing control, not less — and Ramp owns that control layer.

The same logic shapes how Ramp measures itself internally. Dollars blocked and hours saved are posted on walls and in Slack. The scoreboard is built around what the product is supposed to deliver, not just revenue. What you measure is what people optimise for.

On hiring, Glyman is equally direct. Credentials are already priced into a candidate before you meet them. What he hunts for instead is proof of work — spiky, obsessive output in any domain. And as AI lets determined generalists cross boundaries that once required deep specialists, the rigid org structures built around specialisation start to look like scaling bugs rather than features.

## The Core Inversion

### "what if instead of going and trying to get you to be a little bit worse off by spending more than you intended, what if I worked really hard to help you spend less?" [0:04:34]

The contrarian founding insight.
The whole industry agreed on rewards-drive-spend;
Ramp just **asked the opposite question** and built
a different product from it.

### "Ramp was very unusual in that from the get go really what we were trying to sell you was time" [0:54:41]

Banks sell money. Ramp sells **reclaimed hours**.
That's why the scoreboard tracks
dollars blocked and expense reports
automated — not revenue alone.

### "no one is coming to Ramp saying, like, I want to buy a credit card, or can you build me a better expense app? Like, I don't think people should do expense reports." [0:28:34]

The framing shift is the whole business model.
Glyman isn't iterating on a product category —
he's **rejecting the category entirely**.
Most B2B founders never make this move.

## AI as Democratised Expertise

### "large language models have read more lines of code than any engineer alive. They've read more medical case texts than any doctor alive." [0:10:55]

The case for the **determined generalist**.
LLMs collapse the knowledge boundary that used to
enforce specialisation — stubborn curiosity
now substitutes for years of domain training.

### "if I can just ask a good question, I'm the best doctor I've ever been in my life, and I'm not like a doctor at all" [0:10:55]

Glyman's version of the leverage argument —
but grounded in **practice**, not hype.
The unlock is stubbornness, not raw intelligence.

## Ramp's Real Competitor: AI Labs

### "I think that labs are in some sense the most comparable provider of this type of service." [0:54:41]

Bold framing, and probably correct.
Both are selling **automated knowledge work**.
The question is who owns the layer
closest to where the money moves.

### "in a world in which intelligence is very plentiful, in which intelligence is accessible, in which intelligence is cheap, what should you build?" [0:40:32]

The right question once the AI hype
settles. Not "will AI replace us" but
**"what businesses are now possible."**
This reframe is more useful than
90% of AI strategy takes out there.

### "if you assume intelligence will be functionally free in some sense, what is unique and how do we make sure we're adding durable and differentiated value" [0:54:41]

The honest existential question.
Glyman's answer: **own the transaction layer**,
not the intelligence layer.
That's the moat thesis in one line.

## Payments Explode in an Agent World

### "you will have an explosion of payments, whether it's people or agents on behalf of people or agents in general making purchases." [0:40:32]

Ramp's structural bet in one sentence.
More intelligence = more **economic activity** =
more transactions needing governance.
The substrate play, not the model play.

## Measuring What You're Trying to Deliver

Org dysfunction framed as a **communication problem**,

## Hiring for Proof of Work

### "I'm less interested in what is the resume. I'm far more interested in proof of work." [0:15:32]

Clean, **usable hiring heuristic**.
Awards, GitHub activity, something you built —
anything that shows the output, not the credentials.

### "one of the most damaging things you can do to an organization is if there are folks who are getting paid similarly, have similar ownership and are just not pulling their weight." [0:23:52]

**Free-riding destroys standards** from the inside out.
High performers calibrate their effort to what
the organisation actually enforces, not what
it claims to value.

## Key Arguments

1. **Invert the incumbent's incentive**
   Credit cards profit by nudging spend up;
   Ramp wins by actively saving customers money —
   the product is structurally opposed
   to how the industry makes money.

2. **Real competitor is AI labs, not banks**
   Both sell knowledge-work automation;
   Ramp's moat is being embedded where
   money physically moves, not just
   where intelligence is generated.

3. **AI makes payments explode, not shrink**
   More agents acting autonomously =
   more spend needing governance;
   Ramp owns that control layer.

4. **Measure what you're trying to deliver**
   Dollars blocked and hours saved
   are on the wall, in Slack, in pitch decks —
   not just revenue. Scoreboard shapes behaviour.

5. **Design from observation, not requests**
   The Breville "A Bit More" button
   came from watching people toast,
   not from a customer survey.
   Watch what people do.

6. **Hire for proof of work, not pedigree**
   Credentials are priced in early;
   spiky, obsessive output in any domain
   is the signal worth hunting.

7. **Specialisation silos are a scaling bug**
   AI lets determined generalists cross
   domain walls that once required experts —
   rigid org shapes deserve questioning.
