# Notes: The Simple Genius of Rick Rubin

## Summary

Rick Rubin's core method is **reduction**, not production —
strip everything until what remains has nowhere to hide,
then protect whatever magic appears.

That principle started at 18, making hip-hop records
in a NYU dorm room because the existing records
didn't sound like the actual club.
It hasn't changed in four decades.

The more interesting thread: Rubin describes himself
as a **lazy workaholic** — someone who has to force
himself to show up daily, but is completely addicted
to the moment something goes from nothing to great.

Sustaining that over 40 years comes down to:
running on intuition, treating work as diary entries
(not legacy), meditating, and knowing the magic
isn't coming *from* him — he's just in the room
when it arrives.

Relevant to Loom: the whole conversation is a
masterclass in **taste as process**, not talent as trait.

## Quotes

### "If you're stacking a lot of things on top of each other, each one of those things becomes less important."

The arithmetic of attention. Ten elements means each carries one-tenth the weight. This is the load-bearing idea behind everything Rubin does — and it maps directly to product design. Loom's feed should feel like this: fewer signals, each one doing more work.

---

### "The fact that I didn't know what I was doing allowed it to be true to what hip hop was."

The professionals making hip-hop records weren't hip-hop people — they made a Hollywood version of it. Rubin's ignorance was the competitive advantage. Outsider naivety beats insider polish when the insider has lost contact with the real thing. Worth remembering when building Loom — don't over-engineer toward what a "feed app" is supposed to look like.

---

### "I thought the word meant to build up. Like, I think of production as building. And really what I was doing was taking apart and reducing."

At 18 or 19, he renamed his credit from "produced by" to "reduced by" because the word was more accurate. Most people in creative roles default to addition. Rubin made subtraction the explicit job title. That's a values statement, not a technique.

---

### "Sometimes, for the sake of the whole work, removing things about you really love is part of the process."

His ruthless edit framework: if you need to cut 30%, don't whittle down to 70% — cut to 40%, then build back up to 70%. The over-cut forces a cleaner understanding of what actually matters. The second pass, building back from 40%, is where you discover what was load-bearing vs. what was just habit. Applies directly to feature decisions on any product.

---

### "It feels like his entire life is centered around writing words. He's totally preoccupied with that."

Describing Eminem: always has notebooks, always making notes in tiny letters. Asked if he's working on new songs, Eminem said no — just keeping active in the skill set. 90% of what he writes will never become a song. Practice divorced from output. That's what mastery looks like from the inside — not deliberate prep, just compulsion. Most people only work when there's a deliverable.

---

### "My demeanor would be to do nothing, Rick."

He says he has to force himself to work. His natural inclination is inertia. But he does force himself — every day. This is the most honest thing in the whole conversation. The Zen exterior is real, but underneath is someone fighting their own laziness constantly. Productive people tend to be wired this way — the discipline isn't effortless, it's just non-negotiable.

---

### "That moment of discovery where it goes from nothing to something really good. And then the whole rest of the process after that is protecting that because it's super delicate when it happens."

The moment something works is fragile — the rest of the process is about not ruining it. Most people treat the breakthrough as the end. Rubin treats it as the beginning of a protection problem. That's a different and better frame for finishing things.

---

### "If you only wait for inspiration, it won't ever come. Like you have to work and be there and show up."

Being in the practice is a necessary but not sufficient condition. Showing up doesn't guarantee the thing happens — but not showing up guarantees it won't. The fishing analogy earns its keep here. You can't catch fish from your sofa, but you can absolutely spend a whole day on the water and catch nothing. Both are true simultaneously.

---

### "Jimmy is in the banking business and you're in the church business. And that's the difference."

Jimmy Iovine's own words, describing the difference between himself and Rubin: one runs on passion and belief, the other on bottom line and what's good for business. The sharpest two-sentence career taxonomy I've heard. Most people drift between the two modes without ever naming which one they actually are. Rubin has always been church. That clarity is probably half the reason he's lasted.

---

### "I think most of what I do is not really about music. I happen to work in music, but it's not about the music."

His studio, Shangri-La, has the same aesthetic as his records, the objects he buys — it all fits into a worldview, not a genre. The method is transferable because it was never genre-specific. That's why he could go from hip-hop to Johnny Cash to Metallica without losing consistency. The aesthetic is the constant, not the domain.

---

### "Each of those installments are real. So it's always true. It doesn't mean that's who you are forever. It's who you are in that moment."

Treating work as diary entries: no regrets about the past, because that was real at the time. Especially useful for younger artists who treat each thing as a potential magnum opus — a daunting hill that paralyses them. The diary frame removes the pressure of permanence. What you make today is just today's entry. Tomorrow gets its own page. Useful mental model for anyone who over-edits out of fear rather than craft.

## Key Arguments

1. **Less is more, but getting to less is hard work** —
   reduction only works if what remains is critically
   curated; stripping down reveals personality that
   layering buries.

2. **Insider credibility beats professional polish**
   when the professionals have lost contact with
   the actual thing — Rubin's "ignorance" at 18
   was his edge, not a limitation.

3. **The creative process is fishing, not cooking** —
   you can control whether you show up and cast,
   but not whether anything bites; the patience to
   wait without guarantee is the real skill.

4. **Sustained success requires groundedness, not
   talent** — meditation, knowing the magic isn't
   *from* you, and treating work as diary entries
   (not legacy) are what kept Rubin functional
   while others imploded.

5. **Intuition over formula** — because he believes
   humans know almost nothing with certainty, trusting
   gut feel and staying true to personal taste is
   the only coherent operating principle; it also
   happens to produce work that resonates with others.
