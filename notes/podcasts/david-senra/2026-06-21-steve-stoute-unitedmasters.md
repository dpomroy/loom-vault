# Notes: Steve Stoute, UnitedMasters

## Summary

Steve Stoute's central insight is that **culture — not demographics** — is the engine of consumer behaviour.

He proved it first in advertising. Labels and agencies were targeting by race while artists like DMX were already selling in Iowa and Eminem in Harlem. Stoute saw that people organise around shared values, not skin colour, and built Translation around that thesis.

He left a $2M salary to do it. He was explicit that the bet was on the education, not the outcome — the unknown was better than the known, so he ran toward it.

The second business, UnitedMasters, is the same insight applied to music ownership. Labels negotiated Spotify's launch without securing **fan data** for artists — deliberately, Stoute argues, because if artists knew who their fans were, they wouldn't need labels at all. Prince had to erase his own name to escape a contract. That era is closing.

The through-line is ownership. Russ, Bad Bunny, and Ryan Coogler retain their IP and build generational wealth with smaller audiences than label-backed peers. The artists who sign everything away die broke. Stoute's framework for disruption is that culture, technology, and storytelling must converge into one system — mutual empathy between those worlds is the mechanism, not just the idea.

One quieter thread runs underneath all of it: craft. Stoute places Nas and Jay Z alongside Maya Angelou and Shakespeare as writers. The surrounding media noise obscures the literary quality of the words. Managing Nas, he says, is one of the greatest things he has ever done.

## Running Toward the Unknown

### "When the unknown is a better option than the known, run in that direction. Run towards darkness." [0:03:40]

The framing here is useful — it's not reckless risk-taking, it's a **directional bet** based on reading which side of a fork is decaying. Stoute knew the music industry's CD monopoly was structurally broken, so the unknown was the lower-risk option by comparison.

The logic applies cleanly to any decision about staying in a dying system vs. jumping into an unfamiliar one.

### "I was really betting on the education. I sincerely mean that. I did not know what was going to happen at all, but I knew I was going to learn." [0:19:44]

Dropping from $2M to $150K and framing it as **tuition** rather than a pay cut is a clean mental model. Most people can't make that trade because they conflate income with identity.

The equity he dismissed turned out to matter less than the client relationships — McDonald's and Reebok — he picked up during that period.

## Culture Over Demographics

### "Black consumers are the best consumers in the world because they buy products that aren't marketed to them." [0:17:00]

This is a sharp observation about **market inefficiency**: brands were extracting value from Black consumers while giving them nothing in return culturally. Stoute's insight was that closing that gap wasn't charity — it was an arbitrage opportunity.

Madison Avenue left money on the table for decades by treating demographics as monoliths.

### "It's not a TV commercial. It's entertaining content, whether it's a music video… you call it a TV commercial only because you run it on television." [0:22:00]

The **format obsession** of the ad industry was always a distraction from the underlying question: does it hold attention? Stoute collapsed the distinction between music video and ad 20 years before everyone else had to.

This is now table stakes. In 2001 it was apparently a radical enough idea that people pushed back on hiring a music video director to shoot a commercial.

### "Placing bets on emerging subculture is on its path to mainstream culture." [0:57:49]

Bet on the right people driving emerging subculture early, and it's only a matter of time before that becomes the mass-consumed product. This is Stoute's actual investment thesis — pattern-matched from Cash App's early podcast bets. The signal is subculture velocity, not current audience size.

## LeBron and the $10M Bet

### "This 18 year old kid says, no, I'm betting on myself… he walked away from a $10 million check… to go back to the projects in Akron and back to homeroom." [0:29:00]

The LeBron story works as a **proof point for self-belief** at scale — not just personal conviction but a signal about where an entire generation was heading. Stoute reads it correctly as a cultural leading indicator, not just an anecdote.

Worth noting: the $10M was a *signing bonus on top of* the competitive offer. Turning that down is genuinely remarkable.

### "When you're making that margin on that product, the last thing on your mind is who's making money off the glasses… because we're giving it away." [0:33:34]

The **incumbent blindspot** — you don't look for adjacent revenue when the core business is printing money. This is the same dynamic that let Stoute build Translation: the labels weren't ignoring brand deals out of stupidity, they were just too comfortable.

Same pattern plays out in every industry disruption. Comfort kills peripheral vision.

## Labels Withheld the Data

### "The record companies, I believe, never negotiated that when they had that leverage because they didn't even want to have to share that information with the artist." [0:33:36]

The record labels deliberately left fan data on the table — not through incompetence, but to keep artists dependent. That's a structural choice to preserve the middleman, not to serve the product. Worth remembering whenever a platform claims data-sharing is "too complex."

### "If the artists knew who their fans were, they wouldn't need a record company at all." [0:33:36]

200,000 true fans — with direct contact and CRM — is enough to build a serious business through music plus high-margin merchandise. This is the creator economy thesis in one sentence. The label's real product was always access, not A&R.

### "Prince changed his name from Prince to a symbol to get out of a contract because Warner owned his name, image and Likeness." [0:39:59]

He wrote "Slave" on his face — one of the most successful artists ever — because he didn't own the IP that came out of his own brain. This is the analogy Stoute uses to explain the music deal structure to tech investors. It lands because it's absurd, and it's absurd because it's real.

## Ownership as the Only Asset

### "I explained this to Larry Page was the first guy that invested in United Masses. I said, could you imagine if every time a seed investor came in, they actually own the IP and an intellectual property?" [0:42:10]

That's exactly what happens in music — the first check-writer owns your name and likeness. The tech framing is deliberately jarring. Silicon Valley built its entire culture around founder ownership; the music industry built the opposite.

### "He says, you're not getting the movie, you're not getting my intellectual property unless you allow me this movie to revert back to me." [1:04:40]

Coogler's Sinners deal — rights revert to him after a period — is unprecedented at studio scale, with hundreds of millions on the line. Once one person breaks that ceiling, everyone behind them demands the same terms. The ice only needs breaking once.

## Disruption Requires Convergence

### "if you want to disrupt and do something that is groundbreaking, you have to live at the convergence of culture, technology and storytelling." [1:09:25]

The thesis of UnitedMasters in one sentence. Most tech founders skip culture; most culture people skip tech. Stoute's whole edge is that he refuses to let either side stay in its lane.

### "you can get anything done in this world if you're willing to not take credit." [1:09:25]

Bono gave him this line. Obvious when stated, almost never practised. Worth having taped somewhere visible when the politics of a project start eating the project.

### "How you do anything is how you do everything." [1:15:28]

Stoute watched Kobe's locker and his practice routine and saw the same person. The Kobe detail makes this cliché land — a thousand shots *then* an assembly-line defensive drill at 18 or 19 is the evidence, not the aphorism.

## Nas, Jay Z, and the Craft of Rap

### "Nas and Jay Z are in that conversation. They're that good. The way they can paint pictures that you would never. With words like you're watching a film is a phenomenal gift." [1:27:01]

Stoute's point is that the medium obscures the craft — the beats, the clothing, the media create noise around what are genuinely great writers. The Dylan-Kobe-Jay Z mimicry thread Senra draws just before this is one of the better lateral connections in the conversation.

### "I was one of 50 people who tried to see that he was an undervalued asset. It wasn't a unique idea. He chose me." [1:33:23]

Stoute is unusually honest here — he doesn't mythologise the discovery. The insight was obvious; the courage to walk into Queensbridge was the actual differentiator, not genius. Execution beats idea, again.

### "one of the greatest things that I've ever done was be able to manage Nas, one of the greatest writers of all time." [1:27:01]

Said matter-of-factly, not as a boast. Stoute has done Translation, UnitedMasters, brokered Still Dre — and he still ranks *proximity to greatness* above everything. That's a telling value signal from someone who could easily lead with his own accomplishments.
