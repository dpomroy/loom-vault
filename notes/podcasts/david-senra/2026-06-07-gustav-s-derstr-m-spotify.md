# Notes: Gustav Söderström, Spotify

## Summary

Spotify co-CEO Gustav Söderström argues that **first-principles thinking** — not imitation — is what separates durable companies from ones that merely survive.

The episode opens on org design. Söderström says there is no correct structure, only tradeoffs. Apple's famous functional model works, but only because of deep senior tenure that took decades to build. Copy the structure without the trust, and you get politics instead of product.

The business model argument follows the same logic. Subscriptions align incentives with users because the company only wins when users genuinely benefit. Advertising inverts that: it optimises for attention at any cost, and users end up regretting more than half their time on those platforms. Spotify's expansion filter — music, podcasts, audiobooks — passes a simple test: do users feel good about the time spent?

The **GenAI** section is the sharpest part. Söderström sees real danger in AI becoming the most addictive algorithm ever built, because the technology now understands people deeply enough to exploit them. His counter-bet is "premeditated media": letting users set their own filters before they are in the moment, returning control rather than extracting attention.

The final thread is about disruption as opportunity. Spotify beat Apple Music not by waiting for stability but by treating survival as non-negotiable and moving hard during platform shifts. Söderström's rule: market share stays frozen when things are calm, but it moves fast when things change — so that is exactly when to act.

## Org Structure Has No Right Answer

### "There's this truth out there that eventually your org chart shows up in your product." [0:09:25]

The warning behind Spotify's synchronized-swimming model. If you divide the org into swim lanes, the seams appear in the UX. Most companies externalise their internal complexity onto the user and call it a roadmap.

### "Functional orgs tend to dissolve into politics without the tenure. Without the tenure is the key." [0:11:48]

This is the underrated insight: org structure and **leadership tenure** are inseparable. Apple's functional org works because the same people have been building trust for decades. Copy the structure without the tenure and you get Yahoo.

### "The comfort takes a long while to build. You can't expect someone to come in, especially if they come from corporate America and expect them to stand up to you, because usually there are consequences." [1:04:19]

**Psychological safety** doesn't come from policy,
it comes from repeated proof of no consequences.
That takes years — which is exactly why
tenure matters for honest feedback loops.

### "Maybe it's one of the challenges that Apple had with machine learning and AI. On average they were slightly too old to see that probably people in their org knew all about it, were all over, was too late to see it." [1:10:14]

A pointed **diagnosis** wrapped in a disclaimer.
Tenure culture's biggest blind spot: missing
paradigm shifts that younger hires would've
spotted immediately. Apple as the cautionary tale.

## Building One App for 300M Users

### "You can't win. You can only not lose as much." [0:13:31]

Brutal honesty about org design. Söderström isn't selling a framework — he's admitting there isn't one. The goal is to be good at the things that **actually matter** for your company and consciously accept mediocrity elsewhere.

### "We chose to take the pain of doing a single app with all the complexity that comes with that for the benefit of reaching what was then already 300 something million users." [0:19:21]

The podcast lesson: the problem wasn't product quality, it was **distribution**. Seven good podcast apps existed. None had users. Spotify took the organisational pain of a single app because the leverage was already sitting in their install base.

## Time Well Spent as Strategy

### "On many of the big platforms, people regretted almost 60% of the time they spent or more." [0:23:05]

The no-regrets data is the strongest thing in this excerpt. High engagement and enjoyment are not the same metric. Users on the highest-engagement platforms self-report feeling **trapped**. Söderström had assumed engagement implied satisfaction — the survey killed that assumption.

### "We've kept going into these things that are considered by ourselves and users, time well spent. But we never fully articulated that as a strategy externally." [0:23:05]

Worth noting: this was an **instinct first**, strategy second. Music → podcasts → audiobooks → fitness isn't a diversification play — it's a filter. The category has to pass the regret test before it gets resourced.

## Subscription vs Advertising Incentives

### "when you pay with your own money, that is very, very well aligned with optimizing for what is good for the user. Whereas if in a pure advertising based model your incentives are clearly to maximize time spent at any cost." [0:30:28]

The structural argument for subscription.
Not a values statement — a **mechanistic claim** about incentive alignment.
This is the exact lens Loom should apply when evaluating
any feed product's business model.

### "there is a promise for Generative AI to be the most addictive algorithm you have ever heard of, because now we can understand you so deeply, there is potential for darkness over there." [0:30:28]

The clearest warning in the whole excerpt.
Coming from the CPO of a 761M-user platform,
this isn't hand-wringing — it's an insider confirming
the threat is **real and being actively chosen** by some teams.

### "my mission now is to sort of give back control to consumers over these algorithms. And it's contrarian, but I think it's the right bet." [0:33:27]

Worth flagging as directly relevant to Loom's design direction.
If Spotify — with 761M users and a recommendation moat —

### "finally computers understand English. It used to be a small population of about 1 million developers on GitHub who could talk to computers. Now we all can." [0:33:27]

Clean framing of the GenAI shift.
Not the flashiest take, but **the most useful mental model**
for explaining why now is different from prior AI waves.

## Premeditated Media and User Agents

Deciding what you'll consume before your

## How Spotify Beat Apple

### "we bet on three things and they were all basically counter positions to Apple deliberately. One was freemium... The second was personalization... The third was Ubiquiti." [0:54:06]

Textbook asymmetric strategy.
You win against a giant by **finding their structural weaknesses**,
not by outspending them.
Apple's anti-data stance and hardware lock-in were predictable blind spots —
Spotify just had the discipline to bet on them early.

### "for us this was not optional. We either died or we did this." [0:54:06]

The execution variable that strategy frameworks miss.
All three bets above only **landed** because Spotify had no fallback.
Conviction is cheaper when failure isn't survivable.

## Disruption Is When You Move

### "When things are stable, market share, stay stable. You don't eat market share. So when there's change, there's risk, you can lose market share. But that is also when you have the most opportunity to eat market share." [1:12:40]

Clean and **counterintuitive**: volatility is the
product. Disruption isn't the threat —
it's the only window to gain ground.
The calm periods are a trap.

## Key Arguments

1. **No correct org structure** — only tradeoffs.
   Identify what your company *must* be great at,
   optimise ruthlessly for that,
   accept weakness everywhere else.

2. **Tenure is the hidden variable** in high-
   functioning orgs. Apple's functional model
   works because of accumulated senior trust —
   not because the structure is inherently right.

3. **"Time well spent" is both ethics and
   strategy.** Competing platforms trap users
   who regret 60%+ of time there.
   Spotify's expansion filter — music, podcasts,
   audiobooks, fitness — passes the no-regrets
   test, making it durable, not just defensible.

4. **Subscription beats advertising** for
   incentive alignment. Ads optimise for
   attention; subscriptions optimise for value.

5. **GenAI should return control to users**,
   not maximise engagement.
   "Premeditated media" — AI filtering your
   information diet *before* you're in the moment
   — is already possible. Almost nobody does it.

6. **Disruption is opportunity, not threat.**
   Spotify grew fastest during platform shifts.
   The right response to AI uncertainty is to
   move first, not extrapolate from the last
   decade.
