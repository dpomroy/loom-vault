# Notes: Jeff Zalaznick, Co-founder of Major Food Group

## Summary

Zalaznick co-founded Major Food Group and has
run 77 restaurants with 76 still profitable.
The episode traces his path from JP Morgan
analyst → hotel concierge → food content
→ Bastianich apprenticeship → MFG founder.
Core thesis: food first, coherent narrative,
repeat customers over hype. Carbone was built
on one insight — take Italian-American food
seriously with fine-dining ingredients.
MFG now scales that "best version" thesis
across restaurants, private clubs, hotels,
and CPG. Every move — contrarian or fast —
flows from the same root: conviction-matching
beats safe continuity.

## Quotes

### "The restaurant has to tell a complete story. And if it doesn't tell a complete story, then it doesn't work." [0:23:04]

The triad is food → story → fun, in that order. Most operators obsess over the food and ignore the frame around it. The story is what makes people *feel* something — and feeling something is what brings them back.

### "All that matters is that a customer is going to come back. That's what you need to look at and measure. Because coming once is easy." [0:24:00]

First-visit traffic is noise — any new opening gets it. The return rate is the only signal. This is the same logic as LTV in SaaS, just applied to Dover sole.

### "Everyone's excited to go to an opening... but the big question is, after they've checked that off their list, after they've taken their Instagram photo... do they still want to come back?" [0:25:29]

He's explicitly naming viral attention as a trap. Going viral gets you a first visit; it doesn't earn a second. Useful reminder for Loom — distribution ≠ retention.

### "We're not even going to open for lunch because we need to reestablish this as the place you want to be for dinner." [0:19:52]

He killed the thing the space was famous for — power lunch — to build something better. Counterintuitive but financially clean: lunch doesn't drink, dinner does. Constraint as strategy.

### "I texted him, you know, Four Seasons, question mark. And he wrote me right back, like, let's discuss ASAP." [0:13:31]

Two words and a question mark landed a $40M opportunity. The prep was years of earned credibility; the action was a single text. Bias toward asking rather than assuming the answer is no.

### "You always bet on the person that wants it the most." [0:16:17]

Senra attributes this framing to Josh Kushner. Zalaznick confirms it was exactly what happened here — MFG beat more established operators on hunger alone. Wanting it most is a competitive advantage that scales.

### "to have a line around the block, right, you don't necessarily even making something that's that good. It just has to go viral, right? And that lasts a pretty finite period of time." [0:28:22]

Viral demand is **borrowed time**.
The mechanism is distribution, not quality —
and it has a shelf life of months, not years.

### "everyone always says, well, you have. Do you not like that? No, I love it for me. Because the other side of that is that you have a much more educated consumer who's much more interested in spending their money on food and beverage than ever before." [0:28:22]

Good contrarian read. While peers
complained "everyone's a critic," Zalaznick
saw **rising consumer sophistication** as
pure upside for premium operators.
Applies broadly: platform noise
benefits quality if you're already quality.

### "I was sitting there reading, like, Travel and Leisure magazine and, like, Weir magazines, and everyone's looking at me like, what's wrong with this guy?" [0:36:37]

The **signal was already there** in his 20s —
he just hadn't acted on it yet.
When your reading material is wrong for
the room you're in, that's information.

### "I want to be like that guy that I met last night in the restaurant. And on top of that, I'm never going to be the best at this. I can be the best. I don't know yet what it is, but as long as it's something that has food and people surrounding it" [0:36:37]

This is the cleaner version of career
decision-making: find the room where the
**top person looks like someone you want
to become**. The JP Morgan MD was the
anti-model; the restaurateur was the model.

### "My motive was to become him." [0:52:00]

Blunt and honest.
He joined Bastianich's side project
**not for the business** but for the
access and apprenticeship.
Hidden motive, legitimate strategy —
the startup was the tuition fee.

### "I need to be doing this myself, not talking about the people that are doing it. It was that feeling of, like, this is what I'm meant to be doing" [0:49:36]

The website gave him proximity,
which then made the **gap obvious**.
Covering an industry and operating
in it are entirely different callings —
and proximity is what surfaces the difference.

### "What if you took these dishes and gave them the love and respect that people would give when they were cooking fancy French food in terms of ingredients, sourcing, etc. And you applied that to Italian American food" [0:59:07]

The core insight. Everyone else was treating French as the prestige category — he just asked why the same rigour couldn't apply to a different genre. That's a **category arbitrage** move, not a culinary innovation.

### "Fine dining at that point in time had become this very, very, very serious tasting menu. The chef's always right. I'm gonna serve you these five dishes. That's all you can have tonight." [0:59:07]

He's describing the exact thing Carbone was a reaction against. Worth noting: he's not wrong that the no-substitutions era was a dead end — **hospitality** and **chef ego** were in direct conflict and the market eventually agreed with him.

### "I made the menu the biggest menu in Manhattan, right? Because at that point, everyone was giving you these tiny little cards with, like, six ingredients listed on it" [0:59:07]

A deliberate **counter-signal**. Seventy dishes wasn't accidental abundance — it was a manifesto. Every design choice at Carbone was arguing with the competition at the same time as serving the customer.

### "Only because he broke all those lights did it become the quintessential carbon sign that it is." [1:17:36]

Classic pattern: **constraints forced the better outcome**. The original plan was to keep the Rocco sign intact — the vandalism created the layered neon look that became iconic. He had the presence of mind to flip anger into opportunity within minutes.

### "Now you're Netflix with a restaurant, right? You have people paying annual dues and monthly dues every single year." [1:27:33]

The private club pitch is basically: add a **subscription layer** to a notoriously volatile business. Revenue visibility replaces weather-dependency and seasonality. Relevant framing for any product where recurring revenue is possible but not yet the default model.

### "Our goal is just to make you the best version that you've ever had, which is again, that same philosophy goes throughout everything that we do" [1:22:27]

The **Dyson parallel** Senra draws is exact — don't invent new markets, dominate familiar ones through execution. This is a repeatable thesis, not just a restaurant story. The tomato sauce business is the same bet as the veal chop, just in a different format.

### "The food was never any good. So you go there for dinner, but really you just wanted to go there to have a drink and kind of see what was going on." [1:30:57]

The private club market had a **structural blind spot** nobody bothered to fix. MFG's entire ZZ's thesis is just: be the first to actually take the food seriously.

### "Find me two or three restaurants doing $20 million or more and then it's an interesting city." [1:35:04]

Brutally **simple filter** for market entry. Proof of demand already exists — you just have to outperform weaker operators. No guesswork needed.

### "I turned around the Carbone on Collins street, and in 100 days from taking the lease, got it open." [1:40:03]

Speed as **competitive moat** during COVID. Everyone else froze; he moved. That window doesn't come twice.

### "You can spend as much money as you absolutely want, but there's two conditions. Nothing can be moved." [1:40:48]

The constraint is the **strategy**. By freezing the floor plan he unlocked speed and budget simultaneously. Most builders think money buys time — he understood the inverse.

### "That restaurant with the same exact seats, right? I mean, that restaurant does over $30 million of revenue." [1:40:48]

Same **floor plan**, 4x the revenue. The physical asset was never the bottleneck — brand, training, and execution were. Worth sitting with.

### "I think there's no one here teaching people how to do the service right." [1:40:48]

Everyone else saw a **broken market**; he saw an untrained one. That reframe is the difference between walking away and moving in.

## Key Arguments

1. **Repeat customers** are the only signal
   that matters — first visits are noise,
   everything is optimised around return.

2. A restaurant must **tell a coherent story** —
   every detail (chairs, music, menu, uniform)
   reinforces or undermines it, mostly
   subconsciously.

3. **Viral ≠ durable** — social media fills
   seats without quality, but that demand
   decays fast; passion-first operators
   outlast trend-chasers.

4. **Every career step was deliberate
   apprenticeship** — each role chosen
   for what it taught, not what it paid.

5. **Contrarian repositioning beats safe
   continuity** — killing lunch at NYC's
   most famous lunch venue unlocked
   the economics of The Grill.

6. **Market entry is a numbers test**,
   not instinct — find cities where
   $20M+ fine dining already exists,
   then arrive with a stronger concept.

7. **Speed beats perfection** in a wide-open
   market — Miami Carbone opened in 100 days
   by imposing one hard constraint: nothing moves.
