# Notes: Scott Wu, Cognition

## Summary

Scott Wu's defining trait is **competitive obsession** — he runs mental tree-searches on every decision, and losing genuinely offends him. That isn't a personality quirk; it's the engine that drives Cognition.

Cognition's product, Devin, launched at just 13% SWE-bench accuracy. Wu launched anyway, deliberately, to plant a flag. The bet was that staking out "AI as coworker" early would compound faster than waiting for capability parity — and it did.

The deeper thesis underneath Devin is a **autonomous work time** curve that is doubling every few months. A few years ago, AI could sustain uninterrupted work for 10-20 seconds. Now it's hours. Wu's first-principles question is simply: why not days, then weeks, then months?

Enterprise has become 75-80% of Cognition's revenue. The target use-case isn't pure boilerplate (scripts handle that) or genuinely hard unsolved problems (agents aren't ready). It's the middle ground — migrations, version upgrades, complex-but-repetitive backlogs — where every Fortune 500 firm is drowning.

Wu won't sell. Not because the price hasn't been right, but because regret-aversion is his actual motivator. He would only sell if acquisition were the *more* ambitious path. It isn't, so he won't. The long-run vision is that software engineering collapses entirely into natural language — and he wants to be the one who built that.

## Wu's Competitive Operating System

### "It's like a tree search where you're exploring the different options in the decision tree and you're trying to figure out how to lead to victory. That's the only thing I do in my life." [0:00:57]

Wu isn't being dramatic here — he means it literally. The strategy-game framing is useful: it means he's always calculating, not reacting. That's a different cognitive mode to most founders.

### "Salty just means that you take offense to the idea of losing." [0:04:38]

Clean definition. Worth bookmarking. "Taking offense" is a more precise word than "hating" — it implies a moral dimension, like losing is a personal insult. That's a different kind of fuel.

## First Principles Over Pattern Matching

### "We've been spending all this time living in survival mode as a species, and now we're going to be living in creative mode." [0:11:37]

The Minecraft framing is Wu's co-founder's, not his — but he endorses it. It's a genuinely useful mental model. The shift isn't just productivity, it's about what humans spend cognitive cycles on.

### "Rather than any kind of pattern matching, what really matters is just thinking about things from first principles." [0:21:12]

Wu's argument is that pattern-matching works 99% of the time — but this is a 1% moment where the discontinuity is real. The honest question is whether he's right about which category we're in, or whether everyone in a hot sector says this.

## Autonomous Work Time Curve

### "There's the famous METR report which was saying a couple years ago, AI would do about 10 to 20 seconds worth of human work without interruption... And that's just doubled every couple months, basically. And now we're talking about hours of work." [0:21:03]

The METR doubling curve is the most concrete, falsifiable claim in this excerpt. If that rate holds, "days" and then "weeks" of uninterrupted autonomous work are near-term — not sci-fi. Track this number.

### "If you just ask from a first principles question, well, why can't that be days? Or why can't that be weeks or months of work?" [0:22:57]

Wu's point is that extrapolating the doubling curve leads to a structurally different world — one where the bottleneck is no longer execution but knowing what you want. That's the real thesis behind Cognition, and it's more interesting than "faster coding."

## Planting the Flag Early

### "The reason that you are sometimes able to anyway is if you really plant your flag in the ground and put a stake into what you think the future is and you run like hell towards that" [0:37:29]

Startups have no right to exist by default — no resources, no brand. The only lever is directional conviction held early and executed on hard. This is the cleanest articulation of why being "wrong on the details but right on the direction" still wins.

### "The idea that you would work with AI as a coworker rather than as a tool or a chatbot, I think was, I mean, over the last couple years has obviously like really grown" [0:37:17]

Being first to plant that flag — even with a 13%-benchmark product — paid off in brand, recruiting, and customer trust. Timing the narrative correctly matters as much as timing the product.

### "I remember the first time that it did a real task. I could not sleep that night." [0:32:22]

The task was setting up MongoDB — mundane by any measure. But seeing the **exponential curve ahead** from a single success is what drove conviction through the hate cycle. Worth noticing: the insight wasn't "this works," it was "why shouldn't everything be built this way."

## Measuring Output, Not Activity

### "People talk about, oh, yeah, we rank our engineers by how many tokens they're spending. Well, let's try and rank people by how much output they're actually producing" [0:48:59]

Token spend as a productivity metric is the new lines-of-code fallacy. Good push-back from someone with real skin in the game — his own product's adoption depends on customers measuring the right thing.

### "We are just going to care so much more about it... what does it look like to build software end to end at Goldman Sachs or at Mercedes Benz" [0:52:39]

The Spotify/Daniel Ek argument: **focus beats resources** because caring more compounds into product depth that generalists can't match. Direct parallel to Loom — a narrow, deeply-understood problem beats a feature-complete everything tool.

## How Devin Is Built

### "Devin is purposefully meant to be a compound model system... it will dynamically go and choose these models for these tasks" [0:50:28]

Model-agnostic routing — Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, open-source, their own — chosen per sub-task. **Switzerland positioning** means customers trust them to optimise cost/performance without a vendor bias baked in. Architecturally interesting for anything building on top of LLMs: pegging to one model is already a liability.
