# Notes: #420 The Lost Years of Steve Jobs

## Summary

NeXT was the decade that made Steve Jobs worth following.

Jobs founded NeXT in 1985 partly out of **revenge** on Apple. That corrupted motive poisoned priorities from the start — he was running on ego before the company had a product or a dollar of revenue. His self-identity as an artist, not a businessman, meant he consistently chose purity over survival.

The gap between what he preached and what he did was total. He lectured on cost discipline while spending recklessly. He promised team permanence while churning every co-founder. His perfectionism killed every deadline, and the competition moved on without him.

The culture he built made honesty structurally impossible. Engineers lied on timelines. Executives froze. Nobody would deliver bad news to a man who didn't want to be guided in his thinking — he wanted facts, but only facts that confirmed what he already believed. The result was a broken **information environment** that guaranteed operational failure: bad accounting, burned investors, channel stuffing, and a hardware product nobody bought.

The software pivot was obvious to everyone but Jobs. He clung to hardware until Canon's ultimatum forced the decision. The company turned its first profit almost immediately after.

That pattern — being the last to see what was plain to others — repeated enough times to do something useful. Costly, public failure forced genuine humility. He learned to hold talent, absorb hard facts, and stop overplaying his hand. When Apple came calling, those twelve years in the wilderness had produced a fundamentally better operator. Gil Amelio never understood what he was actually buying.

## Why NeXT Was Always Compromised

### "It is probably the defining point of Steve Jobs life because you will see one of the most brilliant entrepreneurs, maybe the greatest entrepreneur to ever live, just make mistake after mistake after mistake."

The defining frame for the whole period. Most people skip to the Apple return. The NeXT years are where the actual character-building happens — and it's ugly.

### "His self identity does not revolve around being a businessman."

Steve frames himself as someone who **builds neat things** — not a businessman. That identity gap explains the $100k logo, the Ferrari factory fantasy, the $10k sofas. He wasn't running a company; he was curating an aesthetic.

## Being the Product Before You Have One

### "Between now and when you have a product, you are the product, my friend. And so you better be nice to people."

Paul Rand — 30 years older, zero patience — cuts through Steve's volatility in one line. This is the kind of feedback only someone un-fireable could deliver. Steve needed more people like this and had almost none.

### "You know what my mistake was? I gave Steve too much dang money. When you have too much money, you just don't have that hunger."

Ross Perot nails it. **Constraint is the forcing
function** — remove it and you get floating
staircases and $10k chairs instead of a
shipping product. Easy money killed the urgency.

## Perfectionism Kills Execution

### "As time went on, the deep shit list started to look less like an existential priority list and more like Steve's personal wish list."

He kept piling on features, adding costs and making last minute demands. The "deep shit list" is a perfect metaphor for the whole NeXT era: **urgency theatre** masking indiscipline.

### "We are over a year late. The competition has progressed more than we expected. Our product is twice as expensive as we imagined. The computer is still not working. You must reset expectations."

Daniel Lewin's memo is a five-bullet death sentence for the company's original thesis. Steve ignored it for months until he saw it himself. That's the pattern: **truth only lands when Steve discovers it personally**.

## The Real Competition: Your Own Ability

### "The most important thing you're competing with is not another company, but it is your own ability to execute."

Steve said this while being blissfully unaware they couldn't execute. Brilliant insight, zero self-awareness. He was diagnosing a disease he was causing.

### "Every operating assumption we had has been proved wrong. The product wasn't ready, the budget has collapsed and the strategy keeps shifting. We are set up for continued failure."

The clearest diagnosis of Next's rot — and it came
from inside. Not one assumption held. This is what
happens when a founder substitutes reality distortion
for actual product-market fit.

## Choosing Purity Over Survival

### "He was choosing purity over survival."

Four words that explain most startup failures.
Steve's aesthetic obsession with hardware
**overrode basic commercial sense** for nearly a
decade. Pretty objects don't pay payroll.

### "After six years in business, NEXT own leadership couldn't agree on what business they were actually in."

Andy Grove walks in, asks one question, and
exposes the whole charade. **No shared definition
of the business** = no coherent strategy.
Six years in. Staggering.

## The Broken Information Environment

### "Steve did not want to be guided in his thinking. He wanted to know what the facts are on which to base something."

Ed Catmull's insight is the practical key to
working with any strong-willed founder. Don't
**push conclusions** — surface facts and let them
arrive there. Took everyone else years to learn
what Ed figured out early.

## What the Lost Years Actually Built

### "Gil failed to grasp what Steve's 12 years in the wilderness had given him. Steve came away not only with better skills for building technology, but better strategies for getting exactly what he wanted."

This is the whole thesis of the excerpt in two
sentences. **Failure was the curriculum.** The
Steve who walked back into Apple was categorically
more dangerous than the one who got fired.
