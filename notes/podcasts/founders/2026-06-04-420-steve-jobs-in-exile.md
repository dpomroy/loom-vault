# Notes: #420 Steve Jobs In Exile

## Summary

**NeXT** is the defining failure of Steve Jobs' career — and the crucible that made him great.

Jobs left Apple in 1985 with money, momentum, and a talent for spectacle. He spent lavishly on logos and furniture before shipping a single unit. Spite toward Apple drove decisions more than any product vision. The result was a company that mistook aesthetic perfectionism for strategy.

The internal culture made things worse. Jobs demanded what he called **full-body absorption** of the company's DNA — but what he got was silence. Engineers lied about timelines. Executives went quiet when he walked in. No honest feedback meant no correction, and the mistakes compounded.

The hardware business quietly collapsed. Channel stuffing, phantom deals, and a shrinking budget exposed how badly every founding assumption had failed. One memo said it plainly: the product wasn't ready, the budget had collapsed, and the strategy kept shifting. Jobs held on anyway — choosing purity over survival — until a single investor threatened to walk.

What saved him wasn't brilliance. It was time. Investor inertia kept the company alive long enough for something unexpected to happen: exile humbled him. The tantrums, the overplaying, the delusion — they slowly burned off. He stopped wanting to be guided and started wanting facts.

When Apple eventually bought NeXT, its outgoing CEO didn't understand what twelve years in the wilderness had actually produced. Jobs came back not just with a better operating system, but with patience, precision, and the hard-won instinct that the most dangerous competitor is your own inability to execute.

## The Defining Failure Begins

### "It is probably the defining point of Steve Jobs life because you will see one of the most brilliant entrepreneurs, maybe the greatest entrepreneur to ever live, just make mistake after mistake after mistake."

The framing that matters: this isn't a story about genius — it's a story about a brilliant person being **wrong**, repeatedly, at scale. That's rarer and more useful to study.

### "Steve wanted grokking from team members, which he describes as full body absorption of the company's DNA."

He preached deep team loyalty and then turned over **every co-founder**. The gap between the ideal he articulated and the culture he actually created is the whole NeXT story in miniature.

## Spite as Strategy

### "Between now and when you have a product, you are the product, my friend. And so you better be nice to people."

Paul Rand — 30 years older, zero tolerance for nonsense — was the only person who cut through early. **Reputation is the first product.** Jobs ignored this completely.

### "The most important thing you're competing with is not another company, but it is your own ability to execute."

Said while the company **couldn't execute at all.** This is the quote that stings most — he could diagnose the disease and still be the cause of it.

### "As time went on, the deep shit list started to look less like an existential priority list and more like Steve's personal wish list."

Scope creep disguised as urgency. He kept **adding features** while the company bled cash and missed every deadline. Classic founder trap: mistaking taste for strategy.

## Money Without Hunger

### "Somehow $100,000 had become the company's standard spending unit, as if Steve's logo had set the baseline for what constituted normal expenses."

This is how **culture actually gets set** — not by what the founder says, but by what the founder spends. The logo fee wasn't just expensive; it was a policy statement.

### "Every operating assumption we had has been proved wrong. The product wasn't ready, the budget has collapsed and the strategy keeps shifting. We are set up for continued failure."

Next's internal diagnosis, brutally accurate.
The rare moment someone in the building
said the quiet part loud — and Steve
ignored it anyway.

### "You know what my mistake was? I gave Steve too much dang money. When you have too much money, you just don't have that hunger."

Ross Perot nails it. Floating staircases
and $10k chairs when you're burning
$39M over budget. Constraints are a
feature, not a bug — Steve needed
scarcity, not a cheque.

## Choosing Purity Over Survival

### "He was choosing purity over survival."

Four words that explain a decade of
bad decisions. The hardware obsession,
the beautiful objects — Steve mistook
aesthetics for strategy. This is the
trap every founder-aesthete risks.

### "Steve did not want to be guided in his thinking. He wanted to know what the facts are on which to base something."

Ed Catmull's insight is the practical key
to working with any high-conviction,
fast-talking leader. Don't push — give
them data and wait. Let them arrive.

## What Exile Actually Built

### "Gil failed to grasp what Steve's 12 years in the wilderness had given him. Steve came away not only with better skills for building technology, but better strategies for getting exactly what he wanted."

This is the thesis of the whole episode.
The exile wasn't punishment — it was
graduate school. Gil thought he was
hiring a chastened entrepreneur;
he was handing the keys to a
fully-loaded version.

### "Steve doesn't know how to do anything but lead."

Shortest quote, biggest warning.
Everyone around Gil saw it coming.
The only person who didn't was Gil.

   Spite-driven strategy — attack ads,

   priorities from the start.

   Every revision delayed delivery, raised
   costs, let competitors catch up.
   Trapped in his own trap.

   Engineers lied about timelines. Execs
   went silent when Jobs entered the room.
   No honest signal = no correction.

4. **Ego dressed as strategy.**
