# Notes: The NFL

## Summary

The NFL is the **largest single sports media property** in the world — ~$23B/year revenue, $228B total franchise value. This episode traces how it got there: from mob football in 1869, through the forward pass, the founding in 1920, the AAFC war, Pete Rozelle's media revolution, the Super Bowl's invention, Monday Night Football, and now streaming + private equity.

The core thesis: the NFL succeeded not by being the best football league, but by being the best at **collective capitalism** — sharing revenue equally, protecting competitive balance, and treating the league as the product, not the teams.

The 2026 update shows the flywheel still spinning: gambling bettors up 30M, Netflix/YouTube deals, Taylor Swift expanding the fan base, and PE firms now paying carry to the league just for the right to invest.

## Quotes

### "On any given Sunday, any team in the league should be able to beat any other team."

This is Bert Bell's mantra — and they literally made a movie with this title. It's the founding philosophy of competitive balance that everything else is built on.

The NFL figured out before anyone else that **drama is the product**, not the team on the field. Every structural decision — the draft, the schedule stacking, revenue sharing — flows from this one idea.

### "Baseball towered above the sporting landscape like a colossus, the unquestioned national pastime, the only game that mattered. Most fans had come to accept baseball's primacy as something immutable, as much a part of the natural order of things as air and water."

This quote from McCambridge's *America's Game* captures exactly how dominant incumbents feel before they get displaced. Baseball wasn't just popular — it felt permanent.

The NFL was a scrappy underdog for decades. The lesson: **"immutable" is just another word for "hasn't been disrupted yet."**

### "pro football robs the great American game of many of its greatest character building qualities. The ideals of generous service, loyalty, sacrifice and wholehearted devotion to a cause are all taken away."

Michigan coach Fielding Yost, 1922. He's partisan, obviously, but this was the genuine prevailing sentiment — that pro football was a moral corruption of the college game.

The NFL had to overcome **cultural stigma**, not just competition. That context matters for understanding why Jim Thorpe as first president, and Pete Rozelle's PR obsession, were such smart moves.

### "The distance between Jim Thorpe as an athlete and any other athlete in the world at that point in time was greater than I think that distance has ever been since."

This is a striking claim. Thorpe won two Olympic gold medals — including in the decathlon, the first time he'd ever competed in it — while also playing professional football and baseball.

Using Thorpe as the league's first president was pure **legitimacy arbitrage**. The NFL's founders were smarter than they looked.

### "radio wets the appetite, television satiates it."

Baseball's operating theory — and why they turned away from TV for so long. It was correct in the short term and catastrophically wrong in the long term.

This is a great case study in **confusing the proximate revenue driver with the actual product**. Baseball protected gate revenue and lost the century. The NFL, having less to lose, experimented — and won.

### "It has to be super polished and it has to be super controlled. Ed Sable's little outfit that would become NFL films is the ultimate embodiment of Rozelle's mindset."

NFL Films started with a suburban dad bidding $5,000 on championship rights. Rozelle didn't create it — he recognised it and let it run, hands-off, as long as the P&L stayed non-negative.

**Narrative control as competitive moat** — this is what the NFL has that no other league has replicated. Every highlight, every slow-motion touchdown, every boom mic on the field traces back to this accidental acquisition.

### "You don't make threats at a time like this. Our answer will be an action. This is not the time to speak."

Al Davis, responding to the Giants breaking the gentleman's agreement by poaching a kicker. Classic Al Davis — maximum menace, minimum words.

Worth stealing verbatim for future negotiations. Also illustrates a key dynamic: **the NFL repeatedly needed external pressure** (AAFC, AFL, Davis, Congress) to do the right thing for itself.

### "the NFL sorted Sundays in America and turned it into a completely different way that people spend their time. And that was shocking."

Nobody predicted this. CBS had a hole in their Sunday schedule and figured football might fill it. What actually happened was the NFL **rewrote the cultural calendar** for 100 million Americans.

Platform businesses do this — they don't just serve behaviour, they create it. The NFL owns Sunday in the same way Google owns "search." The habit is the moat.

### "everything he's doing is through the lens of how do we raise the stature of the league? Not a team, but the league, the NFL as a league."

Rozelle's core insight. Every decision — NFL Films, NFL Enterprises, moving HQ to New York, courting Sports Illustrated, the Hall of Fame — was about the shield, not any individual team.

This is the hardest thing to sustain in any coalition business. **Individual incentives constantly pull against collective stature.** The NFL held it together longer than anyone thought possible.

### "The NFL has built basically no technology, basically no distribution and basically no direct relationship with the audience. And they'll still be fine."

Ben's observation that the NFL outsourced every hard operational problem — broadcast trucks, ad sales, streaming infrastructure — to partners who pay *them* for the privilege.

This is the **ultimate content IP play**. The NFL doesn't make the product; it licences the right for others to make it, and captures most of the margin. Amazon, Google, Netflix are just the new CBS.

### "Communist capitalism is alive and well."

The closing line of the 2026 update — team valuations up 60%, gambling bettors up 30M, PE firms paying carry to participate, and yet the shared revenue model still holds.

The phrase is glib but the insight is real: **the NFL proved that collective ownership structures can outperform pure market competition** when the product is inherently zero-sum. Every team needs the other teams to be viable.

### "Upon any eventual sale or monetization of the ownership stake that private equity would have in an NFL franchise, a portion of the returns on that investment get skimmed off the top and go back to the NFL and then get distributed equally among all 32 NFL team ownership groups."

The NFL invented carry on PE investment in its own franchises. Four approved firms. 10% cap. Silent LP status. And a cut of the upside goes back to the league to be split equally.

This is genuinely novel financial engineering. They didn't just **allow** private equity — they structured it so every owner benefits from every PE exit, regardless of which team the PE bought into. League-first applied to capital markets.

## Key Arguments

1. **The NFL's success is structural, not sporting.** Competitive balance — through reverse draft order, schedule stacking, and shared national revenue — was a deliberate design choice made under competitive pressure from the AAFC and AFL. The game on the field is almost incidental.

2. **Television was the transformative unlock, but the NFL had to be dragged into it.** Baseball protected gate revenue and lost the medium. The NFL, with less to lose, experimented — and Pete Rozelle understood earlier than anyone that TV revenue was theoretically **uncapped** in a way stadium seats never were.

3. **The NFL is a narrative business as much as a sports business.** From Rozelle writing stories for journalists, to NFL Films, to Monday Night Football's production values, to media week — the league has always known that **drama and story** are the product, and football is the delivery mechanism.

4. **Private equity entering the NFL is the most significant structural change in decades.** Four approved firms, 10% cap, silent LP status, and carry flowing back to all 32 owners equally — the NFL applied its league-first philosophy to capital markets and extracted maximum value while preserving competitive balance incentives.

5. **The bear case is real but distant.** Gen Z skews NBA, CTE suppresses youth participation, international expansion is nascent, and local revenue inequality is growing as a share of total revenue — eroding the shared-pool logic that made the league. None of this will bite for a long time, but the flywheel **can** eventually slow.
