# Notes: Formula 1

## Summary

F1 is the world's **most popular annual sport** — 827M fans — yet most Americans only discovered it recently via Netflix.

The story has three acts:

- **Wild West origins:** British RAF vets, Italian luxury, Monaco glamour — chaos held together by cigarette money and cowboy team owners
- **Bernie's 45-year grip:** a London car dealer who centralised power without ever legally owning anything, extracting billions while keeping the sport dysfunctional
- **Liberty's professionalisation:** Fox/ESPN veterans buy in 2017, install cost caps, launch Drive to Survive, and turn famously loss-making teams into $3.6B average-valuation businesses

Why it matters: F1 is now a case study in how a **structurally broken sports property** can be unlocked by the right operator — and how media (Netflix) can do more for fan growth than decades of traditional broadcasting.

## Quotes

### "Formula one is Ferrari, and Ferrari is Formula one. It's that simple."

Said by Bernie Ecclestone about a competitor's team — the man who would go on to replace Enzo Ferrari as the sport's defining power. The quote eats itself. Bernie understood the Ferrari mystique better than anyone and used it as leverage his entire career.

---

### "Adding power makes you faster in the streets. Subtracting weight makes you faster everywhere."

Colin Chapman, 1958. This is the founding insight of F1 engineering — and it still drives the sport's obsession with aerodynamics over raw power. Ferrari ignored it for decades. The teams that internalised it won championships.

---

### "I carry out my business in a very unusual way. I don't like contracts. I like being able to look someone in the eye and then shake them by the hand rather than do it the American way with 92 page contracts that no one reads or understands. If I say I'll do something, I'll do it. If I say I won't, I won't."

Bernie's operating philosophy in one quote. The no-contracts approach wasn't charm — it was **strategic ambiguity**. You can't sue someone over a handshake. This is how he extracted 8% when he'd verbally agreed to 2%, and how he sold something four times he never legally owned.

---

### "There wasn't enough money on that train for me to be involved. I could have done something bigger."

Bernie's response to questions about the 1963 Great Train Robbery — £2.6M stolen. He didn't deny it. He just called it small-time. This is pure Bernie: the denial *is* the brand. And given what he'd go on to extract from F1, he wasn't wrong.

---

### "Bernie Ecclestone was someone who sold Formula 1 four times, has never bought it back, has never lost its control, and still owns it. And do you know the most important thing? He never effing owned it in the first place."

Eddie Jordan's summary of the whole Bernie saga. The most precise sentence ever spoken about F1's governance. This is the central paradox of the entire business — control without ownership, extracted through sheer force of personality and strategic opacity.

---

### "Your problem, Max, is you always want things absolutely clear. And sometimes it's better if things are not clear."

Bernie to Max Mosley, his legal right-hand man and eventual FIA president. This is the operating system of the Bernie era. Ambiguity wasn't a bug — it was how he held leverage over every party simultaneously. Worth remembering when you're tempted to over-document everything.

---

### "Two decades after Marlboro execs looked at F1 drivers and saw the heirs to the American cowboy, Mateschitz recognized them for what they really were, over-caffeinated adrenaline junkies with scant regard for their personal safety. It was a match made in marketing heaven."

Red Bull replacing tobacco as F1's dominant sponsor category is one of the cleanest brand-fit stories in sports marketing. Marlboro sold **aspiration**. Red Bull sold **identity**. The shift also opened the door to younger demographics that Bernie explicitly didn't want — because they "don't buy Rolexes."

---

### "If an insurance company sponsors a team and that team loses, people don't change their insurance company. But when the Red Bulls lose, people get a new drink."

The sharpest articulation of why Red Bull *had* to own a team rather than just sponsor one. Their brand equity is directly tied to performance. This also explains why they run at near-zero profit margin — the racing operation is the marketing department.

---

### "An F1 driver is operating at fighter pilot-like cognitive load for 90 minutes straight. Six Gs under pressure, which means their head weighs like 80 pounds at that moment. Their heart rate is 180 beats a minute or more and they've lost 5% of their body weight during the course of the race."

From Colin Fleming, former Red Bull test driver. The human performance angle is wildly under-covered relative to the engineering. F1 isn't just a car race — it's an endurance sport for the driver's nervous system. This reframes what "the best driver" actually means.

---

### "During the 2019 season, the logistics operation, moving these cars in the circus around, accounts for 64 times the emission of the cars themselves, even when you include practice, qualifying, and races."

The sustainability argument for hybrid engines is **a complete farce** and this stat proves it. F1 flies a fleet of seven 777s between races and sends 300 trucks across Europe. The carbon in the power units is a rounding error. The sport is performing sustainability theatre while the actual problem is the global circus logistics model.

---

### "The percentage of F1 audience that is women went from 7% to there are reports out there that it's like 40% today."

Drive to Survive didn't just grow the audience — it **restructured it**. A 7% to ~40% female fan shift is a category-level transformation, not a margin improvement. Bernie's "they don't buy Rolexes" demographic strategy left 30+ years of fan growth on the table.

---

### "The NFL monetizes a fan at $127 per year, and Formula 1 monetizes a fan at $7 per year."

The single most important number in the whole episode. 830M fans, $7 per fan per year. The bull case for F1 is almost entirely contained in this gap. The structural constraint is inventory — only 22 races — but the upside from US penetration and better broadcast products alone is enormous.

## Key Arguments

1. **Bernie was necessary, but not sufficient.** F1 needed a street-fighter entrepreneur — not a Pete Rozelle employee — to centralise power across a chaotic multi-party global sport. Only someone with skin in the game and zero interest in contracts could have done it. But the same traits that built the sport also capped it at 1990s infrastructure indefinitely.

2. **The cost cap was the single most value-creating intervention in F1 history.** Dropping team spend from $400–500M to a $145M cap didn't just save teams — it manufactured profitability overnight and turned the average team valuation from near-zero to $3.6B. The sport was structurally broken until Liberty forced this through.

3. **Drive to Survive is the most impactful piece of sports media ever made.** It didn't just grow viewership — it created fans who never watch races, shifted the gender split from 7% to ~40% female, and directly generated deals like Oracle's $500M Red Bull sponsorship. The "real product" of F1 is the office politics drama, not the racing.

4. **F1 is a structurally protected business** with network effects (teams + circuits need each other), cornered resource (FIA designation as motorsport's pinnacle), switching costs for all parties, and scale economies too large for any rival series to replicate from scratch. The FOTA breakaway failed for the same reason any breakaway would — you can't agree on how to split the pie.

5. **The monetisation gap is the whole investment thesis.** 830M fans generating $7/fan/year vs. the NFL's $127/fan/year points to decades of underinvestment in US market development, broadcast quality, and fan-facing products. Apple's involvement and new regulations enabling more overtaking are the next two levers.
