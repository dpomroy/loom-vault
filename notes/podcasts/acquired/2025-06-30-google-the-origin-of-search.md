# Notes: Google: The Origin of Search

## Summary

Google's rise was deliberate, not accidental.
Larry and Sergey were commercially ambitious
from day one — the PhD was cover while they
found the right vehicle.

PageRank applied academic citation logic to
hyperlinks and was so superior it **threatened**
every incumbent's page-view ad model — which is
why no one bought it.

Survival came through portal licensing deals,
not ads. GoTo.com invented pay-per-click and
keyword auctions first, but failed to patent
any of it. Google took the model, added
**Ad Rank** (quality × bid), and the AOL deal
gave them the scale to make it work.

The result is a winner-takes-all market that
compounds: more advertisers raise prices AND
cover more queries simultaneously.

## Quotes

### "It wasn't that we intended to build a search engine. We built a ranking system to deal with annotations."

Larry on PageRank's accidental origin.

The insight wasn't "let's build search" —
it was "how do we rank which voices matter."
**Same problem, different domain.** That
reframing is where all the value came from.

### "Probably when I was 12, I knew I was going to start a company eventually. I wanted to make the world better. In order to do that, you need to do more than just invent things."

Larry's stated ambition from childhood.

Kills the "bumbling academics stumbled
into billions" narrative immediately.
**Intentional from age 12** — that's the
frame the whole story needs.

### "The CEO is like, why on earth would we move to your algorithm? I want people to stay on my site. I make money when people stay on my site."

Excite's CEO kills the deal on seeing
BackRub outperform their own engine.

This is the **clearest possible demonstration**
of misaligned incentives destroying a market.
Better product, rational rejection — the
incumbents were optimising for the wrong metric.

### "Google from this point, basically forever was how quickly can we deliver someone something relevant so they can leave Google and I've had a good experience finding what they really wanted?"

Ben's summary of Google's founding
user philosophy.

**Send users away fast** — that became
the entire competitive moat. Counterintuitive
versus every portal, and exactly right.

### "From 1993 to 1996, the web grew from 130 sites to more than 600,000."

Stat from John Battelle's *The Search*.

723% YoY for four years. That rate of
growth is what made **crawling the whole
web feasible** in 1996 but impossible by
1998 — the timing window for PageRank
was genuinely narrow.

### "These companies weren't going to focus on search. They were becoming portals. IE they wanted eyeballs, page views. They didn't understand search and they weren't technology people."

Larry post-rejection, explaining why
no one bought BackRub.

He's not bitter — he's **diagnostic**.
The portals had a coherent strategy,
it just wasn't search. That gap is
what left the door open.

### "He writes a check to Larry and Sergey made out to Google Inc. for $100,000, basically just throws it at them, hops in his car and takes off. Google Inc. does not exist yet."

The Bechtolsheim moment is one of the
great founder folklore stories — but the
detail that the entity didn't exist yet
makes it genuinely funny. Conviction so
high he couldn't be bothered with the
paperwork.

### "Google was forced to do distributed computing because their index file was too large to store on any one machine, no matter how big or fancy it could be."

This is the key structural insight of
the whole episode. The architecture
that became Google's moat wasn't a
vision — it was a workaround.
Necessity and genius are often the
same thing in retrospect.

### "Google's hardware failure was over 10% per year. But the whole system was designed that it didn't matter. It was all just replicated."

This is how you turn a weakness into a
design principle. Most companies would
see 10% failure as a crisis. Google
baked it in as an assumption and built
around it. That's the move.

### "Google can scale, period, but also can scale way more cheaply as search traffic rises and as the index keeps growing and getting bigger than anyone else out there on the market. Once the business model kicks in, this is why Google search has a 87% gross margin on it."

The 87% gross margin doesn't come from
the ad product — it comes from
infrastructure decisions made in a
garage. The business model gets all the
credit; the engineering deserves
equal billing.

### "We don't want to write the business plan in the pitch deck. You write the business plan in the pitch deck."

Larry and Sergey handing their Series A
pitch to employee number nine — a
recent undergrad — tells you
everything about how seriously they
took the monetisation question at this
stage. Brilliant product, zero
commercial instinct.

### "The main Google business, innovative new business model that they're going to pursue here. They are going to sell Google search technology to enterprises so that companies can use the same amazing consumer Google search technology to search their own documents and intranets."

The actual Google Series A pitch was
enterprise search software. Worth
sitting with that. The company that
would invent the most lucrative ad
business in history thought the money
was in selling intranet search to
corporates.

### "Doug, we've never paid so much for so little."

The Sequoia partner's line after Google's Series A produced no revenue and the dot-com bubble burst. The attribution is contested — Moritz, Doerr, Vinod — but nobody wants to own it now. Tells you everything about how close Google came to being written off.

### "In 1999, they shut Google down so that they could serve Netscape's users."

The contrast with today's always-on Google makes this almost absurd. But it was the right call — prioritise the partner deal over your own consumer product when survival depends on it. Most founders would've flinched.

### "Jeff Dean codes it up so that dynamically as users are searching, if there's a query that is related at all to any book in the Amazon library catalog, Google will dynamically generate a text ad saying, go buy this book at Amazon, insert an Amazon affiliate link, and drive traffic over to Amazon."

Validating a billion-dollar ad model through affiliate links is genuinely elegant startup thinking. They didn't just test click-through rate — the affiliate structure gave them **full-funnel data**, impressions through to purchase conversion.

### "Bill had this very radical idea, the best search results should be determined by the free market with dollars. Whoever is willing to pay the most is probably the very best search result for your given query."

Gross was booed for this at TED in 1998. He was also basically right. The insight is that **willingness to pay** is a proxy for relevance in commercial search — spammers can't afford high bids on queries they can't convert.

### "He just thought they were obvious. He just thought, this is the way it should be done. Of course it should be billed per click. Of course there should be an auction and the highest bidder is the one that wins."

Gross didn't patent pay-per-click or keyword auctions because he disclosed them publicly at TED — which put them outside the patentability window. **The idea was so obvious to him he didn't protect it.** One of the costliest assumptions in tech history.

### "What was once criticized as absolutely heretical has come to become basically the dominant model of search and search monetization today."

The irony is sharp: Larry and Sergey initially agreed paid search was unethical, launched AdWords with a visually separated "sponsored" pane to signal purity — and then quietly converged on exactly what Gross built. **The moral high ground evaporated when the revenue model worked.**

### "It's not just fully pay-to-play where if you pay the most, you get placed at the top. But actually we incorporate as part of our ad ranking system how effective your ads are at click-through rate."

This is the move that separated Google from Overture permanently. **Quality signal** beats pure bidding — better for users, better for advertisers, better for Google. Three parties win simultaneously.

### "Click-through rates are a proxy for relevance."

Five words. The entire logic of Ad Rank in one sentence. Worth remembering any time you're building a **ranking system** — revealed preference beats stated preference.

### "It becomes for a set of months, the greatest arbitrage in the history of the Internet was to click on your own ads."

A classic case of **Goodhart's Law** — the metric becomes the target. Funny, but also instructive: CPM + CTR ranking is a broken combo. It's why switching to CPC was not optional.

### "You're betting the company if you do that, and Larry Page responds, we should be able to monetize the pages. If not, we deserve to go out of business."

**Conviction** at its most distilled. This is what separates a founding decision from a management decision. Most people hedge; Page put the company behind a thesis.

### "Not just that unit costs go down, it's that as they scale, their revenue per search actually goes up due to the auction system."

This is the structural moat most people miss. **Increasing returns to scale** on the revenue side — not just the cost side — is extraordinarily rare. Once you see it, you understand why the flywheel is unbreakable.

### "Eventually, it just doesn't make economic sense for a competitor to match your pricing. They literally will run out of money to try to spend the way that you can spend."

The **endgame** of the flywheel. Distribution becomes a weapon competitors mathematically cannot match. Worth mapping this logic onto any marketplace business — who has the highest LTV wins the acquisition war.

### "everyone forgets about the fact that they were so aggressive in distribution deals. They didn't just let people magically find their way to Google."
The "great product wins" narrative is a cope.
Google's **distribution aggression** — toolbars, Dell deals,
Firefox default — was as important as PageRank.
Applies directly to any product, including Loom.

### "the average annual revenue generated by a Google user was $2, but with toolbar, it was $10+ even if you're being conservative."
This is **unit economics weaponised as distribution strategy**.
The $8 delta is a budget you can spend acquiring users —
Google turned ARPU math into a flywheel before anyone
else understood what a flywheel was.

### "Google is paying the maker of that program to include a Trojan horse payload of the Google toolbar, which will then become a Trojan horse that lives in your internet browser"
Google paid third-party software makers to bundle the toolbar — a move the hosts acknowledge is technically "adware" or "spyware" — yet users loved it.
**Uncomfortable truth**: the best-loved products
often got there through methods that sound ugly
when described plainly.

### "Jeff Dean builds AdSense in six weeks. The whole system."
Jeff Dean built the entire AdSense system in roughly six weeks, and by the end of 2003 it was doing over **$1 million a day** in revenue.
That gap between the build time and the revenue scale
is staggering. It's only possible because the
**advertiser pool and targeting already existed** —
AdSense was mostly plumbing on top of AdWords.

### "the thing that led to AdSense… was actually part of the prototyping process of Gmail."
Paul Buchheit was experimenting with pulling ads from Google's database to display alongside Gmail content when Larry and Sergey saw it and asked: "Does this work on websites too?" — and that question became AdSense.
**Serendipity as a system**, not luck.
20% time created the surface area for this collision.
The insight didn't come from a strategy deck.

### "there's a reason why when you look at the legacy of the Google IPO, dual class share structure, great idea. Everybody does it. Dutch auction IPO, not a great idea. Nobody has done it since."
The hosts are blunt: dual-class shares became the template for every major tech IPO; the Dutch auction was a failed experiment nobody repeated.
**Good theory, bad practice** — the auction assumed
rational price discovery, but institutions gamed it
or sat out. The $85 price vs same-day close of $100
proves the mechanism failed on its own terms.

### "these are economies of scale that don't just reduce your costs as you get bigger, but it increases your revenue as you get bigger"

The core insight of the episode. Most scale businesses amortise fixed costs — Google's auction model means more users literally raise the clearing price. That's a structurally different beast.

### "A culture of thinking insanely big mostly by inexperienced, untainted people, that helps you with creativity, that helps you come up with new ideas"

The naivety framing is underrated. Domain ignorance is a feature when you're reinventing a category — expertise anchors you to what's already been tried.

### "studying the way that search played out… This is a 'history doesn't repeat but it rhymes,' and God, does this rhyme"

The AI parallel is the actual reason this episode exists — not nostalgia. Monetisation, distribution, and competitive dynamics from 1996–2002 are replaying now.

### "if Larry and Sergey had met and started working on this a few years earlier, it would've been Yahoo… Then if they'd started a few years later, it would've been too late"

Timing as moat is undersold. The window to build Google was genuinely narrow — a few years either side and the same people with the same ideas lose. Worth holding in mind for AI bets right now.

### "The Google of yesterday, the Microsoft of long ago. That company that's on the cusp of changing the world… where you know you'll kick yourself in three years if you don't jump on the bandwagon now"

The "that company" framing is useful shorthand for identifying once-in-a-decade compounders early. The uncomfortable follow-up question is: which company holds that label today — and is it already obvious?

### "the portals couldn't really pivot to it because they couldn't give up all of their portal ad revenues"

Classic innovator's dilemma, clean example. Yahoo's revenue stream was the thing that made it impossible to compete. A cautionary structure that repeats constantly — including in how incumbents are responding to AI now.

## Key Arguments

1. **Deliberate ambition** — the "accidental
   startup" story is false; the PhD was
   a holding pattern while they built.

2. **PageRank emerged sideways** — from
   annotation ranking, not search; anchor
   text gave richer signal than raw
   academic citations ever could.

3. **Incumbents structurally couldn't buy it** —
   better search destroyed page views,
   making Google's superiority a threat
   to CPM portal models, not an asset.

4. **Portal deals, not ads, kept Google alive**
   — the Yahoo $10M + $7.2M/year licensing
   deal bridged the gap to AdWords.

5. **GoTo.com invented the core model** —
   pay-per-click, self-serve auctions,
   full-funnel accountability — and lost
   it all by demoing publicly before
   filing patents.

6. **Ad Rank was the real innovation** —
   folding click-through rate into bid
   ranking aligned quality with revenue
   and separated Google from Overture.

7. **Winner-takes-all compounding** — more
   advertisers raise keyword prices AND
   fill long-tail queries; the AOL deal
   injected the liquidity needed to
   make that flywheel spin.
