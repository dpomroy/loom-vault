# Structure Shapes Returns: Fund Design and Incentives

These sources converge on a single insight: how an investment vehicle is structured determines what outcomes it can produce, often more than skill. Bogle's cost principle — that investors get precisely what they don't pay for — is the passive-investing version of the same logic Bill Maris quantifies: funds above $750M face a mathematical ceiling that collapses returns, regardless of manager quality. Paul Tudor Jones reframes this around risk management as the foundational discipline, not alpha generation. Vlad Barbalat adds a different angle — permanent capital freed from shareholder dividend pressure enables 'investment hygiene' impossible under conventional fund structures. The through-line is that incentive architecture, not manager brilliance, sets the boundary conditions for what's achievable.
