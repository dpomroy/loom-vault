# Curiosity, Courage, and the Courage to Choose Hard Problems

These sources converge on a counterintuitive point: the bottleneck to great work isn't execution skill but the willingness to choose difficult, overlooked problems in the first place. Paul Graham argues that problem selection matters more than problem-solving — most people apply originality downstream, never questioning what they're working on. Ed Catmull adds the mechanism: difficulty itself is the differentiator, because hard problems force solutions that easy ones never could. Peter Thiel reframes this as a scarcity problem — courage is rarer than intelligence. Where they differ slightly: Barbalat grounds it in craft and iteration rather than boldness, suggesting sustained passion compounds results even without the heroic act of choosing something contrarian from the start.
