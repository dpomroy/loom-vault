# Curiosity, Problem Selection, and Compounding Momentum

These passages converge on a counterintuitive model of exceptional output: the inputs that matter most are upstream of execution. Curiosity isn't just motivational fuel — it's navigational, steering you toward the right problems before you've consciously identified them. Problem selection is framed as even more decisive than problem-solving skill, which challenges the common assumption that brilliance shows up in how you work rather than what you choose to work on. The good-ideas-must-seem-bad argument adds a structural reason why conventional taste fails here: consensus filters out precisely the most valuable territory. Morale then operates as the execution layer — a compounding cycle that makes early wins matter disproportionately, connecting emotional state directly to output quality over time.
