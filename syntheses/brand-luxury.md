# Myth Over Product: How Icons Sell Identity

These sources converge on a counterintuitive truth: the most durable luxury brands sell meaning, not function. Rolex's own tagline admits the watch changes nothing — the wearer does. Ferrari's appeal to wealthy Americans depended precisely on Enzo's artistic aloofness, his indifference to them as customers. What each brand shares is a deliberate inversion: weakness reframed as virtue, product subordinated to myth. Disney adds a third dimension — the synthesis of art and commerce isn't a tension to manage but the actual product. Where these cases differ is in mechanism: Rolex used scarcity and endorsement, Ferrari used mystique and rejection, Disney used total immersive world-building. All three made desirability structural, not accidental.
