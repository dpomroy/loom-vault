# Busyness Hides the Work You're Avoiding

These quotes collectively diagnose a failure mode that's hard to see precisely because it looks like success. Per-project procrastination is invisible — you're busy, just not on the thing that matters. Compounding this, even people who work hard tend to be conservative about which problems they choose, defaulting to conventional targets. The planning problem adds another layer: goal-setting only works for achievements you can articulate in advance, which excludes most genuinely original work. Taken together, the argument is that ambition requires active resistance — to the comfort of busyness, to the social pull of safe problems, and to the illusion that deliberate planning reaches the most important discoveries.

So per-project procrastination doesn't set off the alarms that per-day procrastination does. You're too busy to notice it." — [[notes/articles/2026-05-17-how-to-do-great-work]]

- "People show much more originality in solving problems than in deciding which problems to solve. Even the smartest can be surprisingly conservative when deciding what to work on." — [[notes/articles/2026-05-29-how-to-do-great-work]]
